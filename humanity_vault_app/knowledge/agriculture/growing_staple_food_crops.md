# Growing Staple Food Crops

## Summary

Staple crops - calorie-dense plants such as grains, tubers, and legumes - form the foundation of long-term food security, and growing them successfully depends on a few universal principles of soil, water, spacing, and timing that apply regardless of which specific crop is available.

## Main Content

A staple crop is one that provides a large share of daily calories and can be grown reliably, harvested in bulk, and stored for months. The three broad families of staple crops are grains (such as wheat, rice, maize, millet, or sorghum), tubers and root crops (such as potatoes, sweet potatoes, cassava, or yams), and legumes (such as beans, lentils, or peas), which also help maintain soil fertility by adding nitrogen back into the soil. Note that the specific crops suitable for growing depend heavily on local climate, rainfall, soil type, and growing season length - a crop that thrives in a tropical lowland may fail entirely in a cold highland, and vice versa. Communities should prioritize crops that are already known to grow well in their region, or that closely match their local climate's temperature and rainfall patterns.

Before planting, prepare the soil by clearing weeds and debris, then loosening the top layer of soil to allow roots to penetrate and water to soak in. Adding organic matter such as compost, aged manure, or decomposed plant material improves soil fertility and water retention significantly, especially in soils that have been used for many seasons without replenishment.

Seeds or planting material (such as seed potatoes or cassava cuttings) should be planted at a depth and spacing appropriate to the crop - as a general rule, smaller seeds are planted shallower and closer together, while larger seeds, tubers, and cuttings are planted deeper and farther apart to allow room for roots and growth. Planting in rows, even informally, makes weeding, watering, and harvesting much easier than scattering seed randomly.

Most staple crops need consistent moisture, especially during germination and early growth, but not standing water, which can rot roots and seeds. Watering in the early morning or evening reduces water loss to evaporation. Weeding regularly in the first weeks after planting is critical, since young plants compete poorly with weeds for water, light, and nutrients.

Crop rotation - changing which type of crop is grown in a given plot each season, particularly alternating grains with legumes - helps prevent the buildup of pests and diseases specific to one crop and helps maintain soil fertility over many growing seasons. Relying on a single crop type (monoculture) increases the risk that one pest, disease, or bad season could destroy the entire food supply; growing a mix of crop types spreads this risk.

Harvest timing matters: grains are typically ready when they have dried and hardened on the plant, tubers when the above-ground foliage begins to die back, and legumes when pods have filled out and begun to dry. Harvesting too early reduces yield and storage life, while harvesting too late can result in losses to weather, pests, or shattering (seeds falling before collection). After harvest, proper drying and storage (see "Food Preservation Basics") is essential to prevent spoilage before the next planting season.

## Benefits

- Staple crops provide the calorie base needed for long-term food security, reducing dependence on stored or foraged food alone
- The principles of soil preparation, spacing, watering, and crop rotation apply across many different crop types, making this knowledge transferable to whatever crops are locally available
- Growing a mix of crop types and rotating them protects against total crop failure from a single pest, disease, or bad season
- Legume crops improve soil fertility for future plantings, reducing the need for external fertilizer
