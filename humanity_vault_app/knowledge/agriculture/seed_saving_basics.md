# Seed Saving Basics

## Summary

Saving seeds from healthy plants at the end of each growing season ensures a community can continue growing food in future seasons without depending on outside seed supplies, but seeds must be selected, processed, dried, and stored correctly to remain viable.

## Main Content

Seed saving means collecting seeds from this season's crop to plant in a future season, rather than relying on purchased or donated seed every time. This is essential for long-term food security, since a single missed planting season due to lack of seed can take years to recover from. As with crop selection generally, which specific plants are worth saving seed from depends on what grows successfully in the local climate and region - the principles below apply broadly, but local adaptation is important.

Not all plants are equally suited to simple seed saving. Open-pollinated varieties (including traditional or "heirloom" varieties) reliably produce offspring similar to the parent plant when seeds are saved and replanted. Hybrid varieties, by contrast, often produce offspring that are different from and less productive than the parent plant, so seed saved from hybrids is less reliable. Where possible, prioritize growing and saving seed from open-pollinated varieties.

Select seed from the healthiest, most productive, and most desirable plants - for example, plants that resisted disease, produced well, or matured at a useful time - rather than from weak or diseased plants, since saved seed will tend to carry forward the characteristics of the parent plant over generations.

For grains and legumes, allow seed heads or pods to fully mature and begin to dry on the plant before harvesting, then complete drying in a well-ventilated, shaded area protected from rain and direct sun (which can damage seed viability). For fruiting vegetables, allow the fruit to fully ripen (often beyond the point considered ideal for eating) before removing and drying the seeds. Seeds from wet, pulpy fruit (such as tomatoes) often benefit from a brief fermentation in water for a few days, which helps separate good seed from pulp and can reduce certain seed-borne diseases, followed by rinsing and thorough drying.

Seeds must be completely dry before storage - a seed that breaks cleanly rather than bending or smashing when pressed is generally dry enough. Store dried seeds in airtight containers (such as glass jars, sealed cans, or sealed plastic containers) with a desiccant material (such as a small cloth bag of dry rice or powdered milk) to absorb residual moisture. Label every container clearly with the crop name, variety, and the year it was saved, since seeds can look very similar once dried.

Store seed containers in a cool, dark, dry location - heat, light, and moisture all dramatically shorten seed viability. Most vegetable and grain seeds remain viable for one to several years under good storage conditions, but viability decreases over time, so testing germination (planting a small sample to see how many sprout) before relying on a stored batch for a full planting is good practice. To maintain a long-term seed supply, grow out and re-save seed periodically rather than storing the same batch indefinitely.

To reduce the risk of losing an entire seed supply to a single event (fire, flood, pest infestation), keep seed stores in more than one location, and where possible, share seed with other households or communities so that varieties are preserved in multiple places.

## Benefits

- Removes dependence on external seed suppliers, ensuring planting can continue even if normal supply chains are permanently disrupted
- Selecting seed from the healthiest plants gradually improves a crop's adaptation to local growing conditions over multiple seasons
- Proper drying and storage techniques require only basic containers and materials, no specialized equipment
- Storing seed in multiple locations protects against total loss from a single disaster
