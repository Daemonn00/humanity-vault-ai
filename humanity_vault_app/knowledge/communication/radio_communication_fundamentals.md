# Radio Communication Fundamentals

## Summary

Radios allow communication and reception of information without
relying on the internet or mobile phone networks, making them one of
the most resilient tools for staying informed and in contact during
extended disruptions.

## Main Content

Radio communication works by sending information as radio waves
through the air, which can be received by any compatible radio within
range, without needing cables, satellites, or internet
infrastructure. Two broad categories matter for everyday
preparedness: receive-only radios (for getting information) and
two-way radios (for sending and receiving).

A simple AM/FM radio - especially a battery-powered, hand-crank, or
solar-powered model - can receive official emergency broadcasts,
weather updates, and news from local stations as long as the
broadcasting station itself remains on air. These radios are
inexpensive, require no license to own or operate, and should be kept
with spare batteries or a hand-crank/solar charging option as part of
any emergency kit.

Two-way radios fall into a few common types. License-free radios
(such as FRS - Family Radio Service - radios, often sold as
"walkie-talkies") allow short-range communication, typically up to a
few kilometers depending on terrain, and require no license in many
countries, making them useful for keeping in contact with family
members or a small group nearby. Citizens Band (CB) radio offers
longer range than FRS in open areas and is also license-free in many
countries, historically used by truckers and travelers.

Amateur ("ham") radio offers the longest range and most flexibility,
including the ability to relay messages over very long distances using
repeaters or, under the right conditions, direct long-distance contact.
However, amateur radio legally requires passing a licensing exam in
most countries, because operators share the airwaves and must follow
technical and procedural rules to avoid interference. Where legally
required, obtaining a license before an emergency - not during one -
ensures the operator knows how to use the equipment correctly and
avoid causing harm or confusion on shared frequencies.

Regardless of radio type, a few practices apply universally: keep
transmissions short and clear, state who you are and who you are
calling, listen before transmitting to avoid talking over others,
and agree on specific channels/frequencies with your group in advance.
Radios should be stored with spare or rechargeable batteries, kept dry,
and tested periodically so they are known to work when needed.

## Benefits

- Provides a way to receive critical information when mobile networks and the internet are unavailable
- Enables direct communication between people without dependence on centralized infrastructure
- License-free options (AM/FM receivers, FRS radios) offer no-cost-of-entry resilience for individuals and families
- Encourages advance preparation (licensing, practice, agreed channels) that pays off significantly during an actual emergency
