# Basic Hand Tools and Their Uses

## Summary

A small set of basic hand tools - for cutting, shaping, joining, and digging - covers the great majority of construction and repair tasks needed to build shelters, make furniture, and maintain other tools, and understanding their proper use and care extends their working life indefinitely.

## Main Content

Cutting tools include axes, saws, and knives. An axe is used for felling small trees, splitting firewood, and rough-shaping logs; it should be swung with controlled, full-arm motion, kept sharp (a dull axe is more dangerous than a sharp one because it is more likely to glance off the target), and stored with the head covered or wedged safely so the edge is not damaged and cannot cause injury. A saw is used for more controlled, precise cuts across or along wood grain; let the saw's weight and a steady back-and-forth motion do the work rather than forcing it, which keeps cuts straighter and the blade from binding. A sturdy knife is useful for fine carving, preparing tinder, food preparation, and many small tasks - always cut away from the body and keep fingers clear of the blade's path.

Shaping and smoothing tools include files, rasps, and sandpaper or abrasive stones, which remove small amounts of material to smooth edges, sharpen other tools, or fit pieces together more precisely. A sharpening stone is particularly important, since keeping cutting tools sharp is one of the most effective ways to work safely and efficiently - a few minutes of sharpening regularly is far more effective than trying to restore a badly dulled edge all at once.

Joining tools include hammers, mallets, and items for fastening such as nails, pegs, or cordage. A hammer drives nails or pegs and can also be used with a chisel for shaping wood or stone; grip it near the end of the handle for better leverage and control, and strike squarely to avoid bent nails or glancing blows. Where nails are scarce, traditional joinery (notched and interlocking wood joints) or strong cordage/lashings can join pieces of wood or other materials effectively without metal fasteners.

Digging and earth-moving tools include shovels, picks, and hoes. A shovel moves loose soil and is essential for digging latrines, foundations, and wells (see "Well Digging and Safe Water Access"); a pick or mattock breaks up hard or rocky ground before it can be shoveled. A hoe is useful for both digging shallow trenches and preparing soil for planting.

General tool care extends the life of every tool: clean tools after use to remove dirt, sap, or moisture, which can cause rust on metal parts and rot in wooden handles; store tools in a dry place, ideally off the ground; check handles regularly for cracks or looseness, since a tool head flying off a handle during use is a serious injury risk; and sharpen cutting edges regularly rather than waiting until they are very dull. When using any hand tool, work at a measured pace, keep the work area clear of other people and tripping hazards, and stop if fatigued, since most hand-tool injuries happen when users are tired, rushed, or working in a cluttered space.

## Benefits

- A small set of well-maintained tools (axe, saw, knife, hammer, shovel, sharpening stone) covers most construction, repair, and digging needs
- Proper technique and regular maintenance dramatically reduce the risk of injury and extend tool lifespan, which matters greatly when replacements are not available
- Understanding traditional joinery and lashing techniques allows construction to continue even if metal fasteners run out
- These skills directly support other survival tasks: shelter building, well digging, firewood preparation, and food production
