# Basic Circuits and Battery Power

## Summary

Understanding the basic concepts of electrical circuits - voltage, current, and how batteries, wires, and devices connect together - makes it possible to safely test, repair, and reuse salvaged batteries and simple electrical devices even without specialized equipment.

## Main Content

An electrical circuit is a complete loop that allows electricity to flow from a power source (such as a battery), through a device (such as a light bulb or motor) that uses the electricity, and back to the source. If the loop is broken anywhere - a disconnected wire, a dead battery, a broken switch - no current flows and the device does not work. This is the single most useful troubleshooting concept: when something electrical stops working, the cause is almost always a break somewhere in the circuit, a power source that is no longer providing power, or a failure in the device itself.

Voltage can be thought of as the "pressure" that pushes electrical current through a circuit, while current is the actual flow of electricity, similar to water flowing through a pipe under pressure. Batteries are rated by voltage (commonly 1.5 volts for a single AA, AAA, C, or D battery, and around 3.7 volts for common rechargeable lithium-ion cells). Connecting batteries in series (positive end to negative end, in a row) adds their voltages together - two 1.5-volt batteries in series provide 3 volts. Connecting batteries in parallel (positive ends together, negative ends together) keeps the voltage the same but increases the total available current and capacity, allowing devices to run longer.

Most small batteries and devices are marked with their voltage and, for devices, the voltage and current they require to operate correctly. Using a battery with significantly higher voltage than a device is designed for can damage the device; using a battery with lower voltage may cause the device to not work or work poorly, but is generally not damaging. When salvaging batteries, check that voltage ratings match what a device expects, and inspect batteries for damage, swelling, leaking, or corrosion before use - damaged batteries (especially swollen or leaking ones) should not be used and should be disposed of carefully, away from heat and not punctured.

A simple multimeter (a basic electrical testing tool, if available) can measure voltage, helping determine whether a battery still holds a useful charge, whether a wire connection is making contact, or whether a circuit is complete. Without a multimeter, a battery's general condition can sometimes be roughly judged by connecting it briefly to a small known-working device (such as a flashlight bulb or LED with an appropriate resistor) and observing whether and how brightly it lights - a very dim or no response suggests a depleted or failed battery, though this method is much less precise and should be used cautiously, briefly, and not with unfamiliar or potentially damaged batteries.

When repairing simple devices, common failure points include corroded battery contacts (which can often be cleaned gently with a dry cloth or a small amount of fine abrasive, then dried completely before reinserting batteries), loose or broken wire connections (which can sometimes be re-twisted together and secured, though a proper soldered or mechanically secured connection is more reliable), and failed switches (which can sometimes be bypassed by directly connecting the wires on either side of the switch, though this means the device can no longer be turned off through that switch).

Basic safety: never attempt to open or puncture batteries, especially rechargeable lithium-ion batteries, as they can catch fire or explode if damaged, short-circuited, or punctured. Avoid connecting a battery's positive and negative terminals directly together with a wire or metal object (a "short circuit"), as this can cause the battery to overheat rapidly, even causing burns or fire. Keep batteries away from heat sources and do not leave damaged batteries in enclosed spaces.

## Benefits

- Provides a basic troubleshooting framework (check the loop, check the power source, check the device) applicable to almost any battery-powered device
- Understanding series versus parallel battery connections allows salvaged batteries to be combined to match a device's voltage requirements
- Enables safe assessment and reuse of salvaged batteries and simple electronics, extending the usable life of existing equipment (see "Power Bank Survival Guide" and "Emergency Phone Power Management")
- Basic safety knowledge (avoiding short circuits, handling damaged batteries) reduces the risk of fire or injury when working with batteries
