# Emergency Phone Power Management

## Summary

During a power outage or emergency, extending a phone's remaining
battery life can be critical for staying in contact and accessing
information, and several simple, no-cost settings changes can make a
significant difference.

## Main Content

A phone's battery drains fastest when it is actively searching for a
signal, running the screen at high brightness, or running many
background apps. During an emergency, especially when conserving
power for as long as possible may be necessary, the following changes
can substantially extend usable battery life.

Reducing screen brightness to the lowest comfortable level and
shortening the screen's auto-lock/timeout setting (so it turns off
quickly when not in use) are two of the most effective changes,
because the display is one of the largest power consumers on most
phones. Disabling features that are not needed - such as Bluetooth,
GPS/location services, and automatic app updates or syncing - reduces
background power use.

If a phone is in an area with very weak or no cellular signal, it will
repeatedly increase transmission power trying to connect to a tower,
which drains the battery quickly. In this situation, switching to
airplane mode conserves significant power; if Wi-Fi is available and
useful, it can often be re-enabled separately while airplane mode
remains on. If the phone is being kept on primarily to receive
emergency alerts or occasional calls, periodically turning it on for a
few minutes (e.g., once per hour) to check for messages, then turning
it off again, can preserve battery for much longer than leaving it on
continuously.

Closing background apps, switching to a low-power or battery-saver
mode (available on most modern phones in settings), and avoiding
resource-heavy activities (video, gaming, browsing) all help. If
multiple phones are available and only communication is needed, it can
be more efficient to keep one phone on for receiving calls/messages
while others remain off to conserve their batteries for later.

When charging is available but limited (such as from a power bank or
small solar panel), charging in short bursts to maintain a partial
charge (for example, keeping the phone between 20% and 80%) is
generally better for long-term battery health than repeatedly
draining to 0% and charging to 100%, though in a true emergency,
getting the phone charged enough to make an essential call takes
priority over long-term battery health.

## Benefits

- Extends how long a phone can be used to call for help, send messages, or receive emergency alerts during an outage
- Uses only built-in phone settings - no extra equipment required
- Helps prioritize limited power-bank or solar charging capacity toward the most essential device functions
- Reduces the chance of a phone dying at a critical moment
