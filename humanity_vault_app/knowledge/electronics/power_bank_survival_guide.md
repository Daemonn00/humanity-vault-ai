# Power Bank Survival Guide

## Summary

Portable power banks store electrical energy in rechargeable
batteries and can keep phones, radios, and other small devices running
during power outages, but they need to be chosen, charged, and stored
correctly to be reliable when needed.

## Main Content

A power bank is a rechargeable battery pack that stores electricity
and can later transfer it to phones, lights, radios, and other USB-
powered devices. Capacity is measured in milliamp-hours (mAh): a
typical smartphone battery holds roughly 3,000-5,000 mAh, so a power
bank rated at 10,000 mAh can usually recharge a phone about two times,
while larger 20,000 mAh or higher units can recharge a phone several
times or run small devices for days.

When choosing a power bank for emergency preparedness, prioritize a
capacity large enough for at least two to three full phone charges,
multiple USB output ports (so more than one device can charge, or
several people can share it), and - where possible - a model that
supports solar recharging or hand-crank charging as a backup to wall
power. Built-in flashlights are a useful but secondary feature; the
core value is stored electrical capacity.

Power banks should be charged to full before storing them for
emergencies, and checked periodically (for example, every one to three
months) because lithium-ion batteries slowly lose charge over time
even when not in use. Storing a power bank at around 50-80% charge for
long-term storage (rather than always topping up to 100%) can help
preserve battery health if it will not be used for many months,
though for an emergency kit that may be needed at any time, keeping it
charged and simply testing/recharging periodically is more practical.

Power banks should be stored in a cool, dry place out of direct
sunlight, since heat accelerates battery degradation and, in rare
cases, can pose a safety risk. Avoid storing them in a hot car or near
heat sources. If a power bank becomes swollen, very hot during use, or
visibly damaged, it should be unplugged, not used, and disposed of
according to local battery recycling guidance rather than thrown in
regular trash.

To extend how long stored power lasts during an emergency, prioritize
charging essential devices first (phone for communication, a radio for
information) and use power-saving settings on those devices (see
"Emergency Phone Power Management"). A solar panel attachment, even a
small one, can slowly recharge a power bank during daylight hours,
extending usable power indefinitely as long as there is sunlight.

## Benefits

- Provides a portable, reusable source of backup power for communication and lighting devices during outages
- Solar or hand-crank compatible models can be recharged indefinitely without grid power
- Multiple-port models allow sharing power across a family or small group
- Periodic checking and proper storage ensure the power bank works when actually needed
