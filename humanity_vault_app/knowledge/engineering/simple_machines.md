# Simple Machines

## Summary

Simple machines - the lever, pulley, wheel and axle, inclined plane, wedge, and screw - are basic devices that change the size or direction of a force, and combining them allows heavy or difficult tasks to be accomplished with the materials and muscle power available after a disruption.

## Main Content

A simple machine does not create energy; it changes how a force is applied, usually allowing a smaller force applied over a greater distance to move a larger load over a shorter distance, or to change the direction of the effort. Understanding these six basic types makes it possible to recognize and build practical tools for moving, lifting, and shaping materials.

A lever is a rigid bar that pivots on a fixed point (the fulcrum). Pushing down on the long end of a lever can lift a much heavier load on the short end - the farther the effort is from the fulcrum compared to the load, the less force is needed, though the effort must move a greater distance. A simple pry bar, a seesaw, or a long pole used to lift one end of a heavy object are all levers. Levers are useful for lifting heavy stones or logs, prying objects apart, and as the basis for many tools (such as hammers, which use leverage to drive nails).

A pulley is a wheel with a groove that a rope runs over, used to change the direction of a pulling force or, when multiple pulleys are combined (a "block and tackle"), to multiply force. A single fixed pulley lets a person pull down to lift something up, which is often easier and safer than lifting directly. Combining several pulleys allows one person to lift loads far heavier than they could lift alone, at the cost of pulling a much longer length of rope.

A wheel and axle is a wheel attached to a central rod (axle) that turns together; turning the larger wheel with modest force produces a much stronger turning force on the axle (or vice versa). This is the principle behind a hand crank, a winch, a doorknob, and wheelbarrows - using a wheel and axle to move heavy loads with much less effort than dragging them directly.

An inclined plane is a sloped surface that allows a heavy object to be moved to a higher position using less force than lifting it straight up, at the cost of moving it a longer distance. A ramp used to roll a barrel onto a cart, or a path that zigzags up a hill rather than going straight up, are both inclined planes.

A wedge is essentially two inclined planes joined back-to-back, used to split or separate materials by concentrating force into a thin edge - an axe head, a knife, and a doorstop are all wedges. A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder, converting a turning motion into a straight-line (linear) motion with significant mechanical advantage - used in screws, drills, and jar lids.

In practice, most useful tools and tasks combine several simple machines: a hand-cranked well pump might use a wheel and axle plus a screw; a wheelbarrow combines a lever (the handles) with a wheel and axle. When facing a task that seems too difficult to do with available labor - moving a heavy object, lifting water, or splitting wood - thinking through which simple machine (or combination) could reduce the effort required is often the key to finding a practical solution.

## Benefits

- Provides a mental framework for solving "this is too heavy/hard to move" problems using materials commonly available (poles, ropes, wheels, ramps)
- Requires no manufactured parts - functional levers, ramps, and wedges can be made from wood, stone, and rope
- Forms the conceptual foundation for repairing and understanding more complex tools and machinery
- Directly supports construction, water access, and tool-repair tasks by making heavy work achievable with available labor
