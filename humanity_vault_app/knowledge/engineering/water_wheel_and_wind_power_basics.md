# Water Wheel and Wind Power Basics

## Summary

Water wheels and windmills convert the natural movement of flowing water or wind into rotating mechanical power, which can be used directly for tasks like grinding grain or pumping water, or connected to a generator to produce electricity, providing renewable power without any fuel supply.

## Main Content

A water wheel is a large wheel, typically built with paddles or buckets around its rim, mounted so that flowing or falling water pushes against it and causes it to turn. The simplest type, an undershot wheel, sits partly in a flowing stream with the current pushing against paddles at the bottom of the wheel - this works with relatively low effort but is best suited to fast-flowing water. An overshot wheel, where water is directed from above into buckets near the top of the wheel, uses the weight of falling water and is generally more efficient, but requires a source of water with enough height (head) to fall from, such as a small dam, channel, or natural drop. The rotating wheel's axle (see "Simple Machines") can be connected through gears, belts, or a direct shaft to turn millstones for grinding grain, operate a pump, or turn a generator.

A windmill or wind turbine uses blades (sails or vanes) arranged around a central hub so that wind pushes against them at an angle, causing the hub to rotate. Traditional windmills used large cloth or wooden sails; smaller modern-style designs use a number of angled blades around a central rotor. The mill or rotor must generally be mounted high enough to catch consistent wind, away from obstructions such as trees or buildings that create turbulence, and in many traditional designs, the entire mill structure (or just the rotor) can be turned to face changing wind directions.

Both water wheels and windmills require a way to transfer rotating power to where it is needed. For mechanical tasks (grinding, pumping, sawing), this is often done directly through a shaft, gears, or a system of belts and pulleys (see "Simple Machines"), with gear ratios chosen to increase or decrease the speed and force delivered to the final task - grinding grain typically needs high speed with moderate force, while pumping water often needs steady, lower-speed turning with more force. For electrical generation, the rotating shaft turns a generator (a device that converts mechanical rotation into electrical current using magnets and coils of wire); the electricity produced can be used directly for some loads or stored in batteries (see "Power Bank Survival Guide" and "Basic Circuits and Battery Power") for use when the water flow or wind is not available.

Site selection is critical for both: a water wheel needs a reliable, year-round water source with enough flow or drop to turn the wheel under load - sites should be checked across different seasons, since many streams vary dramatically between wet and dry periods. A windmill needs a location with consistent wind - hilltops, open plains, and coastal areas tend to have more reliable wind than valleys or forested areas, though very high winds can also damage poorly built structures, so designs should include a way to slow or stop the rotor in extreme conditions (such as turning sails away from the wind or applying a brake).

Construction requires attention to the forces involved: a water wheel or windmill under load exerts continuous force on its axle, bearings, and supporting structure, so these parts should be built robustly and use durable materials (hardwood, metal where available) at points of greatest wear, and should be inspected and maintained regularly (see "Basic Mechanics for Tool and Equipment Repair") since a failure under load - a broken blade, a seized bearing - can cause sudden, forceful movement and is a safety hazard. Anyone working near a water wheel or windmill while it is in motion should keep clear of the rotating wheel, blades, and any connected belts, gears, or shafts, since clothing or limbs can be caught and cause serious injury.

## Benefits

- Provides continuous mechanical or electrical power using only the natural movement of water or wind, with no fuel required
- Mechanical power can directly perform useful work (grinding, pumping, sawing) without needing to first convert to electricity
- When connected to a generator and battery storage, provides a renewable source of electricity for lighting, communication, and other needs
- Once built, requires only periodic maintenance rather than an ongoing fuel supply, making it valuable for long-term self-sufficiency
