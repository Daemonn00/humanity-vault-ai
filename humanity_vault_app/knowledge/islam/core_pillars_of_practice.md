# Core Pillars of Practice

## Summary

The Five Pillars of Islam - the declaration of faith, prayer, charity, fasting, and pilgrimage - form the foundation of Muslim practice, and understanding how to maintain the daily and recurring pillars (declaration of faith, prayer, charity, and fasting) helps preserve core religious practice for individuals and communities even without access to mosques, scholars, or normal infrastructure.

## Main Content

The Shahada (declaration of faith) is the testimony that there is no god but God (Allah) and that Muhammad is His messenger. It is the foundation of all other practice and can be spoken at any time, in any place, requiring nothing but sincere belief and the words themselves.

Salah (the five daily prayers) are performed at prescribed times: dawn (Fajr), midday (Dhuhr), afternoon (Asr), sunset (Maghrib), and night (Isha). Each prayer involves a sequence of standing, bowing, and prostration, accompanied by recitation, and is preceded by ablution (wudu) - washing the hands, mouth, nose, face, arms, head, and feet with clean water. If clean water is not available or would be harmful to use (such as in illness or severe water scarcity), tayammum (dry ablution using clean sand, dust, or stone) may be performed instead, by lightly touching the hands to a clean dry surface and wiping the face and hands. Prayer times can be approximated using the position of the sun (similar to the sun-based direction methods described in "Basic Navigation Without GPS or Compass") when clocks or prayer-time references are unavailable: Fajr begins at first light before sunrise, Dhuhr shortly after the sun passes its highest point, Asr in the mid-to-late afternoon, Maghrib at sunset, and Isha after the last light of evening fades. The direction of prayer (qibla, toward the Kaaba in Mecca) can be approximated using general geographic knowledge of one's location relative to Mecca, combined with sun or star-based direction-finding, when no compass or reference is available; communities should agree on a single approximate direction to pray together consistently.

Zakat (obligatory charity) is the giving of a portion of one's wealth or surplus to those in need, traditionally calculated as a small percentage of savings or assets above a minimum threshold, given annually. In situations of scarcity where formal calculation is impractical, the underlying principle - that those with surplus have an obligation to support those without, particularly within their own community - remains central and can be practiced informally through sharing food, supplies, or labor with those in greater need (see "Foundational Ethics and Decision-Making Under Scarcity" for related principles of fair resource-sharing).

Sawm (fasting during the month of Ramadan) involves abstaining from food, drink, and other specified actions from dawn until sunset for approximately 29 to 30 days, based on the lunar calendar. Those who are ill, traveling, pregnant, nursing, elderly, or facing genuine hardship (such as severe food or water scarcity that would endanger health) are exempted or permitted to delay or substitute the fast, with the underlying principle that preserving life and health takes precedence; missed fasts are made up later when circumstances allow, or compensated through feeding those in need if making up the fast is not possible. The lunar calendar can be tracked by observing the moon's phases, beginning each new month at the sighting of the new crescent moon.

Hajj (pilgrimage to Mecca) is obligatory only for those who are physically and financially able to undertake it, and is not something that can be performed locally or substituted - it is included here for completeness as the fifth pillar, but is not a daily or recurring practice that requires guidance for maintenance during isolation.

## Benefits

- Provides clear guidance for maintaining the core, recurring acts of worship (Shahada, Salah, Zakat, Sawm) without access to mosques, clerics, clocks, or calendars
- Sun-based prayer timing and direction-finding connect directly to general survival navigation skills, reinforcing both
- Tayammum (dry ablution) ensures prayer can continue even when clean water is severely limited, without compromising water needed for drinking and sanitation
- Preserves community spiritual practice and cohesion during extended periods of isolation or hardship
