# Traditional Weather Prediction and Seasonal Indicators

## Summary

Long before instruments and forecasts, communities predicted short-term weather and seasonal change by observing the sky, plants, animals, and natural cycles, and these traditional indicators remain useful today for planning planting, travel, and shelter when no other forecasting tools are available.

## Main Content

Cloud patterns are among the most useful short-term indicators. High, thin, wispy clouds that gradually thicken and lower over a day or two often signal an approaching weather change, frequently rain within a day or so. Tall, dark, anvil-shaped clouds building rapidly during the day often signal thunderstorms later that day. A red sky at sunset has long been associated with fair weather the following day in many traditions, while a red sky at sunrise has often been associated with worsening weather later that day - though this rule is most reliable in regions where weather systems generally move from west to east, and should be combined with other signs.

Wind direction and changes in wind can also signal shifts in weather - a sudden shift in wind direction, especially if it becomes gusty or noticeably cooler, often precedes a change in conditions, sometimes within hours. A drop in temperature combined with increasing humidity (a "heavy" or "close" feeling in the air) has traditionally been associated with approaching rain in many climates.

Animal and insect behavior has been observed across many cultures as a seasonal and short-term indicator, though individual observations should be treated as one input among several rather than a sole predictor. Birds flying lower than usual or gathering in unusually large groups, insects becoming more active or seeking shelter, and livestock becoming restless or seeking shelter before a storm have all been widely noted as preceding significant weather changes. Many of these behaviors likely respond to changes in air pressure that animals can sense before humans notice other signs.

Plant behavior provides longer-term seasonal indicators useful for planning planting and harvest timing. The timing of leaf-out, flowering, and fruiting in local trees and plants - observed and recorded year over year - tends to track local seasonal patterns more reliably than calendar dates alone, especially in regions where seasons shift somewhat from year to year. Some plants close their flowers or leaves before rain, responding to humidity changes. Communities that keep informal records of when specific local plants typically flower, fruit, or change with the seasons build up a calendar specific to their own location's climate, which is often more accurate for local planning than generic seasonal assumptions.

Astronomical and seasonal cycles - the changing position of sunrise and sunset along the horizon throughout the year, the length of daylight, and the appearance of certain stars at certain times of year - have been used across many traditions to mark the timing of seasons and guide agricultural activities such as planting and harvesting (see "Growing Staple Food Crops"), particularly in regions with less pronounced weather-based seasonal cues.

These traditional indicators are most reliable when used together rather than individually, and when calibrated to local conditions over time through consistent observation - what holds true in one region or climate may not hold true in another. They provide useful short-term (hours to a few days) warning and longer-term seasonal guidance, but do not replace careful judgment, especially before undertaking activities (such as travel or fieldwork) where being caught in severe weather would be dangerous.

## Benefits

- Provides short-term weather warning (hours to a few days) using only observation, with no instruments required
- Plant and seasonal cycle observations support planning of planting and harvest timing (see "Growing Staple Food Crops")
- Combining multiple independent indicators (clouds, wind, animal behavior, plants) improves reliability over relying on any single sign
- Builds a location-specific body of knowledge over time that becomes more accurate the longer it is maintained and recorded
