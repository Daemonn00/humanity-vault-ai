# Basic CPR Overview

## Summary

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is an emergency procedure used to manually preserve brain function until further measures restore spontaneous blood circulation and breathing in a person who is in cardiac arrest.

## Main Content

This guidance covers CPR for adults and adolescents (roughly age 8 and older); infants and younger children require different hand placement and compression depth and are not covered here.

Before approaching, check that the scene is safe - look for traffic, fire, electrical hazards, or anything else that could harm you or the person needing help. Tap the person firmly on the shoulder and shout to check for a response. If there is no response, shout for help, and call emergency medical services immediately or send someone else to call while you continue. If an automated external defibrillator (AED) is available, send someone to retrieve it or get it yourself if you are alone.

Check whether the person is breathing normally by looking for chest movement for no more than ten seconds. If the person is unresponsive and not breathing, or only gasping, begin CPR immediately. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest, place the other hand on top, and push hard and fast at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute, allowing the chest to fully recoil between compressions.

If an AED arrives, turn it on and follow its voice prompts - it will guide you through attaching the pads and will analyze the heart rhythm, advising a shock only if needed. Continue CPR, alternating with the AED's instructions, until emergency medical personnel arrive and take over, the person shows signs of life (such as breathing normally or moving), or you are too exhausted to continue.

## Benefits

- Can double or triple a cardiac arrest victim's chance of survival
- Helps maintain blood flow to vital organs until help arrives
- Can be performed without specialized equipment
