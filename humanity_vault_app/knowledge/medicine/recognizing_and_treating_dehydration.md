# Recognizing and Treating Dehydration

## Summary

Dehydration occurs when the body loses more fluids than it takes in,
and can become life-threatening if not recognized and treated early,
especially in young children, older adults, and people who are ill.

## Main Content

Mild to moderate dehydration may cause thirst, a dry mouth and lips,
dark yellow urine, reduced urination, headache, tiredness, and
dizziness when standing up. Severe dehydration is a medical emergency
and can include sunken eyes, very dark or absent urine, rapid
heartbeat, rapid breathing, confusion or unusual sleepiness, and skin
that stays "tented" for a moment after being gently pinched. Severe
dehydration requires urgent medical care whenever it can be reached.

For mild to moderate dehydration, the most effective home treatment is
an oral rehydration solution (ORS). To make a basic ORS, mix 6 level
teaspoons of sugar and 1/2 level teaspoon of salt into 1 liter (about
4 cups) of clean, safe drinking water, and stir until fully dissolved.
Sip this solution slowly and frequently rather than drinking it
quickly, continuing in small amounts even if there is vomiting or
diarrhea, since slow sipping is more easily absorbed.

Continue offering small, frequent sips of clean water or ORS
throughout the day, and keep eating small amounts of food if possible,
as this supports recovery. Infants, young children, older adults,
pregnant women, and anyone with severe symptoms (confusion, inability
to keep fluids down, very low urine output, or signs of shock) should
be given ORS while urgently seeking medical care, as their condition
can worsen quickly.

## Benefits

- Recognizing early signs of dehydration allows treatment before it becomes a medical emergency
- The oral rehydration solution recipe uses only water, sugar, and salt - ingredients that are widely available and storable
- Knowing which symptoms require urgent medical care helps prioritize limited medical resources toward those most at risk
