# Sanitation, Hygiene and Disease Prevention

## Summary

Simple sanitation and hygiene practices - safe disposal of human waste, regular handwashing, and keeping food and water clean - prevent the spread of diarrheal disease and other infections that have historically caused more deaths than war or famine.

## Main Content

When normal sewage and water systems stop working, disease spreads quickly through contaminated water, food, hands, and surfaces. Most of this spread can be prevented with a few consistent habits, even without modern plumbing or cleaning products.

Human waste must be kept away from drinking water sources, food preparation areas, and living spaces. If a flush toilet is unavailable, dig a latrine pit at least 30 meters away from any well, stream, or water storage area, and downhill from where people live and gather water if possible. After each use, cover waste with a layer of soil, ash, or leaves to reduce odor, flies, and disease transmission. When a pit becomes nearly full, cover it completely with a thick layer of soil and dig a new one in a different location.

Handwashing with soap (or ash and water if soap is unavailable) at key moments - after using the latrine, before preparing or eating food, and after handling waste, animals, or sick individuals - is one of the single most effective disease-prevention actions available. If soap is not available, rubbing hands thoroughly with ash or sand and rinsing with water still removes much of the contamination, though soap is more effective when it can be made or obtained.

Keep food preparation areas, utensils, and storage containers clean and covered to keep out flies, dust, and pests. Wash fruits and vegetables with treated or boiled water before eating raw, and reheat stored food thoroughly before eating. Clean drinking water (see "Water Purification Using Boiling") should be stored in clean, covered containers separate from any container used for non-drinking purposes.

Anyone with diarrhea, vomiting, or an active infection should use a separate latrine area if possible, wash hands especially carefully, and avoid preparing food for others until symptoms resolve. Soiled clothing and bedding from a sick person should be washed separately and dried thoroughly, ideally in direct sunlight, which helps kill many pathogens.

Solid waste (trash, food scraps) attracts pests and should be buried or burned away from living areas and water sources where possible, rather than left to accumulate.

## Benefits

- Prevents diarrheal disease, which has historically been one of the leading causes of death during disasters and disease outbreaks
- Requires no special equipment - latrines, handwashing, and waste separation can be done with materials available almost anywhere
- Protects water sources and food supplies from contamination, multiplying the value of existing water purification and food storage practices
- Reduces the burden on limited medical resources by preventing illness before it starts
