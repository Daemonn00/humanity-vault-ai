# Wound Care and Bleeding Control

## Summary

Controlling severe bleeding quickly and cleaning wounds properly are
two of the most important first aid skills, preventing both
life-threatening blood loss and dangerous infections.

## Main Content

To control bleeding, first protect yourself if possible by using a
clean cloth or gloves as a barrier between you and the wound. Apply
firm, direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth, bandage, or
the cleanest available material, and maintain that pressure
continuously. If blood soaks through, do not remove the original
cloth - add more layers on top and continue pressing firmly. If the
wound is on an arm or leg and there is no sign of a broken bone,
raise the injured limb above the level of the heart while continuing
to apply pressure, as this can help slow bleeding.

Once bleeding has been controlled, secure the dressing firmly with a
bandage wrapped snugly enough to maintain pressure but not so tight
that it cuts off circulation - check that fingers or toes beyond the
bandage stay warm and a normal color. Do not remove any object that
is embedded in a wound; instead, build up padding around it and
bandage around the object to stabilize it, then seek medical help.

For minor wounds once bleeding has stopped, clean the wound gently
with clean water to remove dirt and debris, then cover it with a
clean dressing and change the dressing daily or whenever it becomes
wet or dirty. Watch the wound over the following days for signs of
infection: increasing redness, swelling, warmth, pus, a foul odor, or
fever. Any wound that is deep, gaping, very large, caused by a dirty
or rusty object, or that does not stop bleeding after several minutes
of firm direct pressure requires professional medical care as soon as
it is available.

## Benefits

- Direct pressure can stop most external bleeding using only basic
  clean materials, with no special equipment required
- Proper wound cleaning and dressing significantly reduces the risk
  of infection in the days after an injury
- Recognizing infection warning signs early allows treatment before a
  minor wound becomes a serious medical emergency
