# Critical Thinking and Evaluating Information

## Summary

When formal institutions, fact-checking, and expert verification are unavailable, a community's ability to evaluate claims, rumors, and advice carefully - rather than accepting or rejecting them based on who said them or how confidently they were stated - becomes essential for making safe decisions and avoiding costly mistakes.

## Main Content

In normal times, much of what people believe is verified indirectly - through institutions, experts, and references that have already done the work of checking facts. When those systems are unavailable or unreliable, every piece of important information - a claim about a water source being safe, advice about which plants are edible, a rumor about conditions elsewhere - may need to be evaluated more directly by the people receiving it.

A first useful habit is distinguishing between direct observation, secondhand reports, and assumptions or guesses. "I saw the bridge collapse myself" is different from "someone told me the bridge collapsed" which is different from "the bridge is probably still standing." Being explicit - even just mentally - about which category a piece of information falls into helps avoid treating uncertain information as if it were certain.

A second habit is asking how a claim could be checked, even partially, before acting on it in a way that would be costly or dangerous if wrong. For example, before traveling a long distance based on a report that a route is safe, are there ways to get a second source, or to send a small scout rather than the whole group? Before eating an unfamiliar plant based on someone's claim that it is edible, are there additional confirming signs (matching a known description in detail, observing animals eating it safely, testing a small amount first) rather than relying on the claim alone?

A third habit is noticing when a claim seems designed to provoke a strong, immediate reaction (fear, urgency, anger) rather than careful thought - this does not automatically make a claim false, but claims that discourage questions, demand immediate action, or come from a single source with no way to verify independently deserve particular caution, especially when the recommended action would be costly, risky, or hard to reverse.

A fourth habit is recognizing the difference between expertise and confidence. A person speaking confidently is not the same as a person who has relevant experience or a track record of being correct about similar things in the past. In small communities, it is often possible to recall whether a particular person's past predictions or advice on a topic turned out to be accurate, and to weight their input accordingly - while remaining open to the possibility that anyone, including a normally reliable person, can be wrong about a specific claim.

A fifth habit is being willing to update a belief when new, credible information contradicts it, rather than treating an initial judgment as fixed. This is especially important for decisions that can be revisited - if a decision was made based on the best available information at the time, and better information becomes available, changing course is not a failure of the original decision, but a sign of sound ongoing judgment.

Finally, group decision-making benefits from deliberately seeking out disagreement before settling on a conclusion - asking "does anyone see this differently, or know something that contradicts this?" - rather than assuming agreement means a claim has been verified, since groups under stress can converge quickly on a shared belief that turns out to be wrong (see "Lessons from Historical Civilization Collapse and Recovery" for examples of how groups can reinforce mistaken shared beliefs).

## Benefits

- Reduces the risk of costly or dangerous decisions based on unverified rumors, exaggerated claims, or confidently-stated guesses
- Provides practical habits (distinguishing observation from report, seeking partial verification, noticing urgency-based persuasion) usable without any institutions or technology
- Encourages tracking which sources of information have proven reliable over time within a community
- Supports better group decision-making by deliberately surfacing disagreement before acting on shared assumptions
