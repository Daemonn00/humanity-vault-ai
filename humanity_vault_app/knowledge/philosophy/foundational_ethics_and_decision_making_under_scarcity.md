# Foundational Ethics and Decision-Making Under Scarcity

## Summary

When resources such as food, water, medicine, or shelter are severely limited, having a shared, fair, and consistent way of making hard decisions helps a group avoid conflict, distrust, and choices that cause more harm than necessary.

## Main Content

Scarcity forces choices that would not normally need to be made: who receives limited medical care first, how food or water is divided when there is not enough for everyone, or who takes on dangerous tasks. Without some shared framework for these decisions, they tend to be made unevenly, in the moment, and under stress - often based on whoever is loudest, most powerful, or most afraid - which erodes trust and can fracture a group exactly when cooperation matters most.

A useful starting point is the principle of triage, long used in medicine: when resources cannot help everyone, prioritize based on who can be helped most effectively with the resources available, rather than simply "first come, first served" or favoring particular individuals. This means recognizing that treating the most severely affected first is not always possible if doing so would use all available resources without saving them, while treating those with moderate, treatable needs first may save more lives overall. Applying this honestly, and explaining the reasoning to the group, helps people accept difficult outcomes even when they are painful.

Fairness and consistency matter as much as the specific rule chosen. A group that agrees in advance on how decisions will be made - even an imperfect rule, applied consistently and transparently - tends to hold together better than a group with no rule, or with rules that change depending on who is asking. Where possible, decisions affecting the whole group (such as rationing food or water) should be made openly, with input from those affected, rather than by one person in private. This does not require formal voting or institutions; even a habit of explaining "here is the situation, here is what we have, here is how we propose to divide it, does anyone see a problem with this" builds legitimacy.

Protecting the most vulnerable - children, the elderly, the sick or injured, and those unable to advocate for themselves - is a widely shared ethical principle across cultures and traditions, and should be an explicit part of any group's approach to scarcity, not an afterthought. At the same time, those doing essential physical labor (water collection, security, medical care) may need somewhat more food or rest to remain able to perform that labor, which itself benefits the whole group - balancing these needs honestly, rather than ignoring either consideration, is part of fair decision-making.

Honesty about the situation - including bad news, such as dwindling supplies - is generally better for group cohesion than concealment, even though it can be difficult to deliver. People who feel misled tend to act unpredictably or stop cooperating once they discover the truth, often at a worse moment than if they had been told earlier.

Finally, decisions made under extreme scarcity should be revisited as conditions change. A rule that was necessary during an acute crisis (such as strict rationing) should be relaxed as soon as it is safe to do so, and the group should acknowledge when sacrifices were made so that fairness is restored over time where possible.

## Benefits

- Provides a shared framework for difficult decisions, reducing conflict and arbitrary or biased choices during crises
- Triage-based prioritization helps resources do the most good when they cannot meet everyone's needs
- Transparent, consistent decision-making builds trust and cooperation, which are themselves critical survival resources
- Explicit protection of vulnerable group members reflects values shared across many cultures and helps preserve a community's cohesion and self-respect through hardship
