# Basic Navigation Without GPS or Compass

## Summary

When GPS devices, phones, and compasses are unavailable, the sun, stars, landmarks, and simple route-tracking habits can still provide reliable direction-finding, helping a person travel safely and find their way back to a known location.

## Main Content

The most reliable low-tech direction-finding method uses the sun. In the morning, the sun rises roughly in the east, and in the evening it sets roughly in the west, giving a rough east-west reference line at those times. A more precise method is the shadow-stick method: place a straight stick upright in flat ground, mark the tip of its shadow, wait 15 to 20 minutes, then mark the new shadow tip - the line between the two marks runs roughly east-west, with the first mark closer to west and the second closer to east in locations north of the equator (this reverses south of the equator). Standing with the first mark to the left and the second to the right gives an approximate north-facing direction. At night, in the Northern Hemisphere, the North Star (Polaris) can be found by locating the Big Dipper constellation and following the line formed by its two outer "pointer" stars upward to the next bright star, which is Polaris and indicates true north closely. In the Southern Hemisphere, the Southern Cross constellation can be used: extend a line along its long axis about four and a half times its length to find a point close to true south.

Landmark navigation means choosing a distant, easily recognizable feature - a mountain peak, a distinctive tree, a ridge line, or a tall building - and using it as a reference point to maintain a straight line of travel, since walking without a fixed reference tends to result in unintentional curving (most people drift in one direction over long distances). Before setting out, identify a landmark in the direction of travel and a different landmark behind you in the opposite direction; periodically check that both remain in their expected positions relative to your path. In flat or featureless terrain, two people can walk in a line, with the person behind directing the person in front to stay aligned with a chosen reference point.

Route tracking - keeping a mental or written record of the direction and approximate distance traveled - makes it possible to retrace steps or estimate a return path. Simple techniques include counting paces (a "pace" is typically two steps, and most adults travel roughly 1,000 to 1,300 paces per kilometer on flat ground, though this varies by person and terrain), noting the general direction of travel at each turn (e.g. "we walked toward the sun, then turned left at the fallen tree"), and leaving small, deliberate markers (such as piles of stones, broken branches, or marks on trees) at decision points so the route can be retraced if needed. Writing down or sketching a simple route map, even roughly, is far more reliable than relying on memory alone, especially after several hours or in stressful conditions.

Other simple direction-finding clues include: moss and vegetation growth patterns (in the Northern Hemisphere, north-facing surfaces often receive less direct sunlight and may be damper or have more moss, though this is unreliable on its own and should never be the sole method used), the direction prevailing winds typically blow from in a known local area, and the position of sunrise/sunset relative to known geographic features (such as a mountain range or coastline) observed over previous days.

## Limitations and Safety Considerations

These methods provide approximate direction, not precise position, and accuracy decreases with cloud cover, dense forest canopy, featureless terrain, or unfamiliar environments. No single method should be relied upon alone - cross-check direction using at least two different techniques (for example, sun position and a distant landmark) whenever possible. Before traveling into unfamiliar terrain, if at all avoidable, inform others of the intended route and expected return time. If becoming lost, in many situations it is safer to stay in place near a recognizable location (especially if others know the planned route) rather than continuing to travel and risk becoming more disoriented, particularly as light fades or weather worsens.

## Benefits

- Provides a way to maintain direction and avoid traveling in unintentional circles when electronic navigation is unavailable
- Sun and star-based methods require no equipment and can be learned and practiced in advance
- Route-tracking habits (pacing, markers, simple sketches) make it possible to retrace a path back to shelter, water, or other survivors
- Cross-checking multiple simple methods reduces the risk of relying on a single inaccurate signal
