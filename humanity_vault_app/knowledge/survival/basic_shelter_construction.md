# Basic Shelter Construction

## Summary

A simple shelter that protects against wind, rain, and cold can be
the difference between life and death, since exposure to the elements
can cause dangerous body heat loss within just a few hours.

## Main Content

Choose a shelter site on dry, level ground, away from dry
streambeds (which can flood suddenly), dead trees or large branches
that could fall, and the very top of exposed ridges (which are
windier and more exposed to lightning). If possible, pick a spot near
useful resources such as building materials and, if needed, water -
but not so close to water that insects or flooding become a problem.

A simple and effective shelter is the lean-to. Find or place a sturdy
horizontal pole (the ridge pole) supported at both ends by trees,
forked sticks, or a rock, at roughly waist to chest height. Lean a row
of long branches or poles against one side of the ridge pole at an
angle, spaced close together, to form a sloped wall that blocks wind
and rain from one direction. Build this wall facing away from the
prevailing wind.

Once the frame is built, cover it with smaller branches, leaves,
bark, grass, or any other available material, working from the bottom
up so that each layer overlaps the one below it like roof shingles -
this helps rain run off rather than soak through. Add a thick layer
of dry leaves, grass, or other insulating material on the ground
inside the shelter to lie on, since the ground draws heat away from
the body quickly.

Keep the shelter small - just large enough for the people using it -
since a smaller enclosed space is easier to keep warm with body heat
alone. If a fire is used near the shelter entrance for warmth, follow
fire safety practices: keep it a safe distance away, never build it
inside an enclosed shelter, and ensure good ventilation.

## Benefits

- A lean-to shelter can be built with no tools other than what is found in the immediate environment
- Proper site selection avoids common hazards such as falling branches, flash floods, and excessive wind exposure
- Ground insulation and a small enclosed space dramatically reduce heat loss compared to sleeping in the open
