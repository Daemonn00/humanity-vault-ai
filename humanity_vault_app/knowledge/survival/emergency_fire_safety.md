# Emergency Fire Safety

## Summary

Fire is essential for warmth, cooking, and water purification, but an
uncontrolled fire is one of the fastest-moving dangers in a survival
situation - basic fire safety practices prevent injuries, property
loss, and the loss of a vital resource.

## Main Content

Before lighting any fire, clear a wide area down to bare soil or
rock, removing dry leaves, grass, and other flammable material for at
least one meter in every direction. Build the fire only as large as
needed for its purpose - a small, well-fed fire is easier to control
and uses less fuel than a large one. Always keep a supply of water,
sand, or loose dirt within reach before lighting a fire, so it can be
smothered quickly if it spreads.

Never leave a fire unattended, even for a short time, and never build
a fire inside an enclosed space (tent, vehicle, or sealed room)
because of the serious risk of smoke inhalation and carbon monoxide
poisoning. If cooking or warming a fire indoors is unavoidable, ensure
there is an open window or vent for airflow at all times.

If clothing catches fire, stop moving immediately, drop to the
ground, cover the face with the hands, and roll back and forth to
smother the flames - running will make a fire burn faster. For a
grease or fat fire, never use water, as this can cause the burning
fat to splash and spread; instead, smother it by carefully covering
the pan with a lid or damp cloth, or use sand or dirt if available.

When finished with a fire, extinguish it completely: pour water over
the embers, stir the ashes, and pour more water until everything is
cool to the touch and no steam or hissing is heard. A fire that looks
out but is still hot underneath can reignite later or in wind.

## Benefits

- Clearing a burn area and keeping water or dirt nearby prevents most fires from spreading out of control
- Knowing the stop-drop-roll technique can prevent serious burns if clothing catches fire
- Properly extinguishing fires after use prevents reignition, which is a common cause of accidental wildfires and structure fires
