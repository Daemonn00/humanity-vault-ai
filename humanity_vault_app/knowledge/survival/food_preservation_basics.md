# Food Preservation Basics

## Summary

Preserving food extends the time it can be safely stored and eaten by
slowing down the bacteria, mold, and enzyme activity that cause
spoilage, using simple methods such as drying, salting, smoking, and
fermenting.

## Main Content

Drying removes the moisture that bacteria and mold need to grow. Thin
slices of meat, fruit, or vegetables can be dried in direct sunlight
and wind, or near (but not directly over) a fire, until they become
hard or leathery. Food should be cut into thin, even pieces and spread
out with airflow on all sides, and protected from insects and rain
while drying. Properly dried food should bend or crack rather than
feel moist.

Salting and curing draw moisture out of food and create an
environment where most harmful bacteria cannot survive. Meat or fish
can be packed in a generous layer of salt, or soaked in a strong
salt-water brine, for a period of time before drying or smoking.
Vegetables such as cabbage can be layered with salt to draw out their
liquid as part of fermentation (see below).

Smoking combines drying with the antimicrobial effects of wood smoke.
Meat or fish that has been salted is hung over a small, smoky fire
(using green or damp wood to produce more smoke than flame) for
several hours to days, until it is firm and dry. Smoked food should
still be cooked thoroughly before eating unless it has also been fully
dried and cured.

Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria to preserve food while also
making some nutrients easier to digest. A common method is to submerge
vegetables (such as cabbage, cucumbers, or carrots) completely in a
salt-water brine in a clean container, keeping them weighted down
under the liquid and away from air, and left at a cool room
temperature for several days to weeks until they taste pleasantly
sour.

Whatever method is used, store preserved food in a cool, dry, dark
place in clean, pest-proof containers, and check it regularly. Discard
any preserved food that develops an off smell, unusual color, slimy
texture, or visible mold (other than the surface mold expected on some
cured items), as these can indicate spoilage or dangerous bacterial
growth.

## Benefits

- Drying, salting, smoking, and fermenting all extend food storage life using only basic tools, salt, and heat or sunlight
- These methods reduce dependence on refrigeration or packaged food, which may not be available
- Preserved food provides a stored energy reserve for periods when fresh food is scarce, such as winter or after a poor harvest
