# Water Storage and Long-Term Water Safety

## Summary

Storing water safely and in sufficient quantity ensures a reliable
supply of safe drinking water during emergencies, and proper
containers, rotation, and treatment practices keep stored water safe
to drink for long periods.

## Main Content

A general guideline used by emergency preparedness organizations is
to store at least one gallon (about 3.8 liters) of water per person
per day, covering both drinking and basic sanitation needs, with a
minimum of a three-day supply and a two-week supply preferred where
possible. This means a family of four should aim to store at least 12
gallons (about 45 liters) for a three-day supply, scaling up
accordingly for larger households or longer storage goals. Additional
water should be set aside for pets.

Water should be stored in clean, food-grade containers specifically
designed for water storage, such as polyethylene plastic containers
labeled as food-safe (commonly marked with a "2" or "HDPE" recycling
symbol). Containers that previously held milk, juice, or other
beverages with sugars or proteins are not suitable for long-term water
storage because residue can allow bacteria to grow even after
cleaning. Containers should be rinsed with a small amount of unscented
household bleach solution before filling, then rinsed with clean
water.

Stored water should be kept in a cool, dark place away from direct
sunlight and away from toxic substances such as fuel, pesticides, or
cleaning chemicals, since some plastics can absorb odors and chemical
vapors over time. Commercially bottled water can typically be stored
as packaged, following the manufacturer's guidance, and generally
remains safe well beyond any printed date as long as the seal is
intact and storage conditions are good.

For water stored at home in containers (not commercially sealed),
rotating the supply every six months to a year is a commonly
recommended practice - using the stored water for everyday purposes
and refilling with fresh water - to keep the supply fresh, even though
properly stored treated water can often remain safe well beyond this
if containers remain sealed and undamaged.

If stored water becomes cloudy, develops an odor, or its safety is in
doubt (for example, after a container has been opened for a long time
or storage conditions were poor), it should be treated again before
drinking - boiling for at least one minute (see "Water Purification
Using Boiling") is the most reliable method, and unscented household
bleach (typically 6% sodium hypochlorite, at a ratio of about 1/8
teaspoon per gallon of water, doubled if the water is cloudy) can also
be used, followed by stirring and waiting at least 30 minutes before
drinking.

In addition to stored drinking water, identifying and learning to
treat water from local sources (rainwater collection, wells, rivers,
or lakes) provides a renewable backup once initial stored supplies are
used, but any water from an unknown source should always be treated
(filtered and/or boiled or disinfected) before drinking.

## Benefits

- Ensures a known, safe supply of drinking water is available immediately during an emergency, before other water sources can be assessed or treated
- Reduces the risk of dehydration and waterborne illness during disruptions to normal water supply
- Simple container selection, storage, and rotation practices keep stored water safe for long periods at minimal cost
- Complements active purification methods (boiling, disinfection) for a complete water safety strategy
