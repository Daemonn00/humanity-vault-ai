## Claim B4: Compost pathogen-reduction time/temperature

**Planned article wording (Article 2, Building and Maintaining Soil
Fertility at Scale):** to reduce pathogen risk, a manure compost pile
should be kept between 131°F and 170°F (55-77°C) for at least 3 days if
using an in-vessel or static aerated-pile system, **or** for at least
15 days, with the pile turned at least 5 times, if using a windrow
(open-pile) system - the method most accessible without specialized
equipment.

**Exact source passage read (eOrganic, "Making and Using Compost for
Organic Farming," https://eorganic.org/pages/18567/making-and-using-compost-for-organic-farming,
quoting USDA National Organic Program regulatory language), directly
opened and read 2026-07-19:**

> "Maintained a temperature of between 131°F and 170°F for 3 days
> using an in-vessel or static aerated pile system" **or** "Maintained
> a temperature of between 131°F and 170°F for 15 days using a windrow
> composting system, during which period, the materials must be
> turned a minimum of five times."

- [x] **The Founder has personally read this source passage and
      confirms it supports the claim as written above**, including
      that the two methods genuinely carry different time
      requirements and both must be represented (not collapsed into a
      single "3 days" rule). **CONFIRMED by the Founder, 2026-07-19.**
      Final approved boundary: in-vessel/static aerated-pile systems
      maintain 131-170°F (55-77°C) for 3 days; windrow systems
      maintain 131-170°F (55-77°C) for 15 days with at least 5
      turnings. These figures must never be collapsed into one
      universal composting rule, and apply specifically to the
      confirmed pathogen-reduction context documented above -
      vermicomposting must not be presented as following these same
      time-temperature rules.

## Claim B6 (new): Raw manure pre-harvest application interval

**Planned article wording (Article 2, Building and Maintaining Soil
Fertility at Scale):** raw (uncomposted) manure applied directly to
growing land should not be used within 120 days of harvest for a crop
whose edible portion touches the soil (e.g., root vegetables,
low-growing leafy crops), or within 90 days of harvest for a crop
whose edible portion does not touch the soil - composting the manure
first (see B4) removes the need for this waiting period.

**Exact source passage read (eOrganic, same page as above), directly
opened and read 2026-07-19:**

> "Incorporated into the soil not less than 120 days prior to the
> harvest of a product whose edible portion has direct contact with
> the soil surface or soil particles" **or** "incorporated into the
> soil not less than 90 days prior to the harvest of a product whose
> edible portion does not have direct contact with the soil surface or
> soil particles."

- [x] **The Founder has personally read this source passage and
      confirms it supports the claim as written above**, including
      that this is presented as a widely-used, science-informed safety
      practice (drawn from a real regulatory standard) rather than as
      binding law in every jurisdiction - per this task's instruction
      to keep the pack general rather than a jurisdiction-specific
      regulatory manual. **CONFIRMED by the Founder, 2026-07-19.**
      Final approved boundary: 120-day pre-harvest interval when the
      edible portion has contact with the soil or soil particles;
      90-day pre-harvest interval when the edible portion does not.
      These figures must be presented with their actual context and
      must not be generalized into a universal manure-handling rule
      beyond this confirmed boundary.
