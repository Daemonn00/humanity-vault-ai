# Accessibility, Safety, and Dignified Use of Sanitation Facilities

## Summary

A sanitation facility that works for an able-bodied adult may not work
- or may not be safe - for everyone else; designing for accessibility,
privacy, and safety from the start makes facilities usable and
dignified for the whole population they serve.

## Main Content

Space and layout affect who can actually use a facility. A transitional
or mobile latrine unit needs a minimum usable floor space of roughly
120 by 120 centimeters, though 180 by 180 centimeters is a better
target where it can be achieved - a cramped unit is difficult or
impossible for anyone using a mobility aid, carrying a young child, or
simply larger-bodied. The door itself matters too: at least 90
centimeters wide, opening outward rather than inward so it doesn't
reduce the usable space inside, fitted with a lock or handle that's
easy to grip.

For a wheelchair user specifically, more detailed space is needed:
roughly 80 by 130 centimeters of clear floor space beside the seat to
allow a transfer from wheelchair to seat, plus a turning space of about
150 by 150 centimeters to maneuver the chair itself, along with a
grabrail mounted at approximately 80 centimeters above the floor. As a
planning target across a facility or camp, roughly 1 in every 10
latrines should be built fully accessible to this standard, rather than
treating accessible design as a single token unit.

Distance from where people sleep also affects real usability and
safety, and this is a different measurement from how far a latrine
needs to sit from a groundwater source (see "Latrine Types and
Construction for Camp and Emergency Settings" for that separate
figure). As a walking-distance target, latrines should be no more than
roughly 50 meters from shelters in the earliest, short-term phase of an
emergency, tightening to no more than about 30 meters once a settlement
is more established - closer facilities are used more consistently and
create less risk for anyone walking to them, especially at night.

Reducing the risk of gender-based violence at sanitation facilities is
not a matter of any single fix - it takes several measures working
together. These include building separate, gender-segregated
facilities; fitting working locks so a facility can be used privately;
providing adequate lighting at and around the facilities; and ensuring
that the routes and distances people travel to reach both water points
and sanitation facilities are direct and reasonably short (planning
distances for reaching a water point are covered in "Assessing
Community Water Needs and Protecting the Source"). Lighting alone,
without the other measures, is not enough to make a facility safe -
these considerations work as a set, and a plan that only addresses one
of them leaves real gaps in the others.

Beyond these specific figures, every facility should have separate
male and female sections, a working lock on each unit, and enough
visual privacy that no one can be seen from outside while using it.
These are baseline expectations that apply everywhere, regardless of a
facility's exact design or construction method.

## Benefits

- Gives concrete space and layout figures for accessible latrine
  design, rather than leaving accessibility as a vague aspiration
- Distinguishes facility-to-shelter walking distance from the separate
  groundwater-siting distance, so both get planned for correctly
- Frames gender-based-violence risk reduction as a set of coordinated
  measures, avoiding the false sense of security that comes from
  relying on a single fix like lighting alone
- Establishes baseline privacy and segregation expectations that apply
  to every facility, not just accessible ones
