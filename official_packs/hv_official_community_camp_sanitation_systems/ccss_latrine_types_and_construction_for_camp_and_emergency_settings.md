# Latrine Types and Construction for Camp and Emergency Settings

## Summary

Which latrine technology fits a camp or emergency setting depends on
how much time, ground, and material is available; a basic pit or
trench latrine is the most common choice, but siting, ground
conditions, and construction detail all affect whether it works well.

## Main Content

Several latrine technologies are used across different phases and
conditions. In the immediate phase of an emergency, chemical toilets
or dug trench and pit latrines are the technologies generally used
first. Bucket or elevated toilets raise the containment above ground
level and are specifically useful where the water table is too high to
dig safely. Packet latrines (a bag or container a person uses and then
seals, with no digging required) are another option for settings where
digging isn't possible. Trench and pit latrines - a dug hole or
channel, usually with a simple slab or seat over it - are generally the
most common choice once digging is possible, because they are fast and
cheap relative to other options. Composting or urine-diverting designs
separate and treat waste on-site rather than simply containing it, and
are a further option in the same technology range.

Because trench and pit latrines are the default choice in most
settings, their limits matter. They are not suitable everywhere: a high
water table or unstable, rocky ground can make digging unsafe or make
the pit prone to collapse or flooding. Where either of those problems
apply, an elevated, chemical, or packet-based option (see above) is a
better fit than trying to force a dug pit into unsuitable ground.

Where digging is possible, dimensions matter. A shallow trench latrine
is typically dug roughly 20 to 30 centimeters wide and about 15
centimeters deep - enough for a single use before it's covered - while
a deep trench, meant to serve for longer, can be dug to a depth of up
to about 2 meters. A camp or trench latrine should also be sited with
real distance from any groundwater source such as a well, borehole, or
spring - roughly 15 to 30 meters is a reasonable planning distance for
this. This siting distance is separate from the vertical clearance a
latrine needs above the water table itself, which is covered in
"Shelter Site Selection and System Planning" as part of settlement-wide
site planning - both distances matter, and neither substitutes for the
other.

During use, a pit doesn't need to be filled completely before it's
retired. A common practical guideline is to stop using a pit and begin
backfilling once waste has filled to within roughly 20 centimeters of
the underside of the slab or seat, leaving enough buffer that the
facility can still be safely closed rather than overflowing.

One well-documented weakness of a basic pit latrine is flies and odor.
A Ventilated Improved Pit design reduces both problems significantly
through two connected mechanisms working off the same vent pipe:
continuous airflow moving up and out through the pipe carries odor away
from the latrine rather than letting it build up inside, and flies that
hatch in the pit are drawn toward the light at the top of the vent -
when they try to escape toward it, a screen across the opening traps
them rather than letting them back into the latrine or out into the
open. For this to work, the vent pipe needs to be relatively wide -
at least 11 centimeters across on the inside - and tall enough to clear
the latrine structure itself, extending more than 30 centimeters above
its highest point. This is an upgrade to how a pit latrine is built and
ventilated, not a separate technology, and it reduces flies and odor
rather than eliminating them outright. See "Operation, Maintenance, and
Decommissioning of Sanitation Systems" for how this connects to keeping
a latrine usable over its working life.

## Benefits

- Matches latrine technology choice to real ground and time
  constraints, rather than defaulting to a dug pit everywhere
- Gives concrete construction and siting figures for trench and pit
  latrines, the most commonly used option
- Explains the specific mechanism behind fly and odor control in a
  Ventilated Improved Pit, so the upgrade can be understood and applied
  correctly rather than copied without knowing why it works
