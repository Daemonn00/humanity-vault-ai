# Solid Waste Collection and Disposal at Community Scale

## Summary

Household trash and refuse - separate from human waste - still needs a
planned collection and disposal system at community scale, with
distances, ratios, and a clear boundary around what belongs in
ordinary waste versus what needs specialized handling.

## Main Content

Getting solid waste away from where people live starts with how far a
household has to carry it. As a planning target, a communal collection
point should be no more than roughly 100 meters from a household;
during the initial response phase of an emergency, this is tightened
further, to no more than about 10 meters, since people have less
capacity to manage waste on their own in those first days. Communal
collection itself is generally planned around one roughly 100-liter bin
for every 10 families, sited at least 15 meters from where people live
so it doesn't become a nuisance or a hazard on its own.

How waste moves from collection points onward depends on the size of
the population being served. Hand-carts are a reasonable transport
method for populations of roughly 10,000 to 20,000 people within about
a 1-kilometer radius; beyond that scale, motorized trucks generally
become necessary, once the population being served exceeds roughly
50,000 people.

Where waste is disposed of by burial, siting the pit matters. A waste
burial pit should be sited at least 30 meters from the nearest dwelling
and from any water source, at least 1.5 meters above the water table,
and covered daily with soil to control odor, pests, and disease risk.
This is its own distinct siting requirement for solid-waste burial -
it is not the same measurement as, and should not be confused with,
the separate distance figures used for latrine siting or for
settlement-wide hazard distances covered elsewhere.

Not all waste generated in a community belongs in this ordinary refuse
system. Health-care waste - anything generated in the course of
medical care - is a mixed category: roughly 85 percent of it is
general, non-hazardous material that can be handled like ordinary
refuse, but the remaining 15 percent is hazardous and falls into
several distinct categories, including infectious waste, pathological
waste, sharps, chemical waste, pharmaceutical or cytotoxic waste, and
radioactive waste. The key principle is segregation: keeping that
hazardous fraction separated out from general community waste and
burial systems, rather than allowing it to be mixed in as if it were
ordinary trash. This article does not cover how to treat or dispose of
hazardous health-care waste itself - that requires specialized handling
beyond the scope of a general community waste system - only that it
must be recognized and kept separate from everything else described
above.

When waste has to be burned rather than buried, incineration should be
treated as a last resort rather than a routine method. It should be
done downwind of where people live, with plastics removed beforehand,
since they burn poorly and release harmful smoke. Informal or
uncontrolled burning in particular often fails to reach a temperature
high enough to fully break down what's being burned, which means it
creates air-pollution hazards without reliably solving the underlying
waste problem.

For planning purposes, rough reference capacity figures are useful for
sizing disposal sites in advance: about 6 cubic meters of burial
capacity per 50 people per week, or about 3 cubic meters of landfill
design capacity per 200 people per week. These are planning
approximations to size a system in advance, not fixed requirements
every site must meet exactly.

## Benefits

- Gives concrete distance and ratio targets for collection points, bins,
  and transport method, scaled to population size
- Establishes waste burial-pit siting as its own clear figure, reducing
  the risk of confusing it with other distance rules used elsewhere in
  camp planning
- Draws a clear, actionable line between ordinary community waste and
  the hazardous fraction of health-care waste that must be kept
  separate from it
- Gives planning-capacity reference figures to help size a disposal
  system before it's built, rather than discovering capacity problems
  after the fact
