# Assessing Land and Planning a Resilient, Diversified Food System

## Summary

Before a group commits real effort to growing food together, it helps
to look honestly at what land and resources are actually available,
choose more than one kind of crop to grow, and think about how seed
will be kept going from one season to the next - not just for one
family's garden, but for everyone relying on the same plots of land.

## Main Content

Assessing whether a piece of land can support food production for a
group starts with the same basic questions a single household would
ask - soil quality, access to water, and how much sunlight the site
gets - but at a larger scale, where a poor choice affects more people
and is harder to correct later. Soil that has been used for many
seasons without anything added back to it, ground that floods or
stays waterlogged, or a site too far from a reliable water source all
make food production harder before a single seed is planted. There is
no fixed amount of land that will feed a given number of people: how
much space is actually needed depends heavily on group size, what
people plan to eat, and how productive the local soil and climate
turn out to be, so it should always be worked out from the specific
situation on the ground rather than assumed in advance.

Once a site is chosen, growing more than one kind of crop, rather than
relying on a single crop across all the available land, meaningfully
protects a group's food supply. A pest, disease, or bad season that
affects one crop is far less likely to affect every crop at once, and
rotating which crop is grown in a given plot has also been observed to
increase yields by lowering how much pest and disease pressure builds
up on that plot over time (see "Growing Staple Food Crops" for the
rotation technique itself) - diversification is that same
risk-spreading idea applied to what an entire group chooses to plant
across its land, not just how one plot is rotated season to season.

Keeping a reliable supply of seed for future seasons is just as
important as growing this season's food, and a group can go further
than any single household managing its own seed. Building on the
household-level seed-saving techniques already covered elsewhere (see
"Seed Saving Basics"), a community seed bank pools seed from multiple
households into shared, better-protected storage. Community seed banks
are commonly organized around an elected or agreed-upon committee that
sets and manages the rules for depositing, withdrawing, and
replenishing seed, so the system reflects what the community actually
needs and everyone understands how it works. Seed contributed to the
bank is cleaned, dried, and checked for germination before it goes
into storage - the same care a household would take with its own
seed, just organized for many contributors at once. A shared seed bank
is also more resilient than any single household's private store: seed
kept and managed by a group, in one well-run location, is far less
likely to be lost entirely than seed kept separately by families who
may each face their own individual setback.

Assessing land for growing food is a different question from assessing
land for where to put shelters or a settlement - keeping structures
dry and away from hazards is its own planning problem, covered
separately (see "Shelter Site Selection and System Planning"). Growing
food and housing people are both real planning needs a community will
face, but they call for different questions about the same land, not
the same checklist answered twice. Access to water for growing is
covered separately as well (see "Water for Growing: Irrigation
Planning at Community Scale"), since irrigation planning is its own
substantial topic once a site has actually been chosen.

Some approaches to combining food production with other systems - such
as raising fish and growing plants together in one connected setup -
depend on more specialized equipment and infrastructure than the
general land- and crop-planning approach covered here, and are not
addressed in this article.

## Benefits

- Gives a group a realistic way to judge whether a site is workable
  before investing real effort, without assuming one universal amount
  of land will do
- Spreads risk across multiple crops so one bad season, pest, or
  disease doesn't threaten the entire food supply at once
- Extends household-level seed saving into a shared system that is
  more resilient than any single family's private seed store
- Keeps land-for-growing and land-for-shelter as clearly separate
  planning questions, so neither gets answered with the other's
  assumptions
