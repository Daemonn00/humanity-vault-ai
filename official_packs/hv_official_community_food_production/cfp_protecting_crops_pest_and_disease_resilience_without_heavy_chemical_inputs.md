# Protecting Crops: Pest and Disease Resilience Without Heavy Chemical Inputs

## Summary

Protecting crops from pests and disease doesn't have to start with
chemical pesticides - a combination of prevention, biological and
physical methods, and low-toxicity treatments, with chemical control
reserved as a last resort, can meaningfully protect both growing crops
and stored food.

## Main Content

Integrated Pest Management is an approach that considers every
available method of pest control together, rather than reaching for a
chemical pesticide by default. It combines biological methods
(encouraging or introducing organisms that prey on pests), cultural
methods (growing practices that make pests and disease less likely to
take hold in the first place), and physical methods (direct,
non-chemical removal or exclusion of pests), with chemical treatment
kept as one option among several rather than the first response. The
core idea is that a healthy, well-managed growing system needs less
chemical intervention overall, not that chemicals are never
appropriate.

Growing more than one crop and rotating what's planted in a given plot
from season to season - already covered as a way to build overall
resilience (see "Growing Staple Food Crops" and "Assessing Land and
Planning a Resilient, Diversified Food System") - is also one of the
most effective cultural pest-management practices available. Pests and
diseases that build up around one crop in one place have a much harder
time persisting when that crop isn't grown in the same spot again the
next season, and a diverse planting is a harder target for any single
pest to spread through than a large block of a single crop.

For food already in storage rather than still growing, a few
low-toxicity, plant-based options exist to protect it from pests.
Vegetable oil - including common oils such as peanut or palm oil -
applied to stored grain at a small rate, roughly 5 to 10 milliliters
per kilogram of grain, has been used to help protect stored grain from
certain pests. **This specific rate applies to protecting grain that
is already harvested and in storage - it is not a treatment for pests
on growing plants in the field, and should not be used that way.**
Other low-toxicity, traditionally used materials for protecting stored
seed and produce include wood ash, neem leaves, and chili extract,
used as a physical or repellent barrier rather than a measured
chemical dose.

This article does not attempt to identify specific pest species or
diagnose particular plant diseases - doing that reliably usually
requires either direct local experience with the specific pest and
crop, or help from someone who has it. What this article offers
instead is a way of thinking about pest and disease management -
preventing problems through diversity and rotation, favoring
non-chemical methods first, and using chemical treatment as a
deliberate choice rather than an automatic first step - that holds up
regardless of which specific pest or disease a group actually
encounters.

## Benefits

- Reduces dependence on chemical pesticides that may be scarce,
  expensive, or unavailable
- Reuses the same crop-diversification and rotation principles already
  useful for other reasons, rather than requiring a separate
  pest-management strategy from scratch
- Gives a real, sourced treatment option for protecting stored grain,
  clearly scoped to that specific use
- Offers a way of thinking about pest management that holds up even
  without being able to identify the exact pest involved
