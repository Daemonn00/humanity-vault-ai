# Shelter Site Selection and System Planning

## Summary

Before any shelter is built, choosing and laying out the site it sits
on shapes everything that follows. For a group or settlement, not just
a single household, the same hazards that threaten one shelter -
flooding, unstable ground, wind exposure - become planning questions
that affect many people at once.

## Main Content

A site's slope matters for keeping it dry: a gentle grade of roughly 2
to 4 percent lets rainwater drain away naturally, while a site that is
close to flat collects standing water and a site steeper than about 10
percent is difficult to build on and erodes easily. Groundwater matters
too - a water table at least 3 meters below the surface gives enough
depth to protect a water supply from contamination, and any toilet pit
should stay at least 1.5 meters above the water table for the same
reason.

Planning space for a group is different from planning space for one
family. A commonly used comprehensive planning figure is around 45
square meters per person, covering shelter plots, roads, and shared
facilities; a minimum of about 30 square meters per person (not
counting any agricultural land) is the floor before conditions become
genuinely cramped, and going much below roughly 29 square meters per
person is generally discouraged. These are planning targets for laying
out a site in advance, not guarantees of comfort or a hard rule that
every real settlement meets.

Spacing between shelters is itself a safety measure, not just a
comfort one: a minimum of about 2 meters between structures - ideally
twice the height of the structure - helps keep a fire or a structural
collapse in one shelter from immediately reaching the next one. At a
larger scale, a firebreak of roughly 30 meters is a commonly used
planning figure for roughly every 300 meters of built-up area,
breaking a settlement into sections a fire cannot easily cross (see
"Thermal Management, Ventilation, and Fire Safety in Shelter Systems"
for what happens once a shelter is occupied and heated).

A settlement also needs to be sited at a safe distance from hazards
that affect everyone living there: general waste sites are commonly
kept at least 1 kilometer away, sites with hazardous waste specifically
at least 5 kilometers away, and ecologically protected areas at least
15 kilometers away. Military installations and similarly dangerous
locations are also kept at a distance, commonly at least 1 kilometer.
Natural water sources are given their own protective buffer, commonly
around 50 meters, to prevent both drowning risk and water
contamination. These are settlement-scale planning distances for
whoever is choosing where an entire camp or settlement goes - they are
not advice for where an individual family should camp for a night.

Protecting a shared water source or a whole settlement site from
contamination follows the same underlying principle already true for a
single family's shelter: keep it away from anything that could
contaminate it, and away from ground that floods or collects debris
(see "Basic Shelter Construction" for the same principle applied to
one shelter). A settlement-scale site simply applies this reasoning at
a larger scale, with more people depending on getting it right.

Beyond the site itself, planning for water points, toilets, health
facilities, and schools follows commonly used population ratios - for
example, roughly one toilet per 50 people is a reasonable emergency-
phase target, with water points, health facilities, and schools each
planned at their own appropriate ratio for the population size (see
"Water Treatment at Scale" for the water-system side of this
planning, and "Sanitation, Hygiene and Disease Prevention" for the
sanitation side).

Rather than trying to solve every part of a site's planning at once,
a useful approach is to address the highest-priority risks first -
drainage and contamination hazards before comfort and convenience -
and improve the rest incrementally as time and resources allow.

## Benefits

- Gives a group or settlement realistic planning figures for space,
  spacing, and distance-from-hazard, rather than guessing
- Catches drainage, contamination, and fire-spread risks at the
  planning stage, before they become harder problems to fix later
- Applies the same hazard-avoidance thinking already used for a single
  shelter, scaled up to protect many people at once
- Supports planning in priority order, so a site doesn't need to be
  perfect before it starts being safer than an unplanned one
