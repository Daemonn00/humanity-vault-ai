# Thermal Management, Ventilation, and Fire Safety in Shelter Systems

## Summary

Staying warm in a shelter and avoiding fire or carbon monoxide
poisoning are closely connected problems: many of the ways people try
to heat a shelter without normal utilities - generators, fuel-burning
devices, fireplaces, space heaters - carry a real risk of causing the
exact harm they're meant to prevent, if used incorrectly.

## Main Content

**Fuel-burning devices must never be used indoors.** Gas stoves and
ovens, barbeque or charcoal grills, gasoline generators, and any
vehicle must never be operated indoors, in an attached garage, or in
any poorly ventilated or enclosed space, because of carbon monoxide
poisoning risk. This is a categorical rule, not a conditional one -
carbon monoxide is colorless and odorless, and poisoning can occur
whenever a fuel-burning appliance or machine is not vented properly,
whether or not it seems to be working normally.

**Generators specifically need to be kept well away from the
shelter.** A portable generator must be run outdoors, far from the
building, and specifically away from doors, windows, vents, and any
area where people are sleeping - never inside a shelter or garage, and
never on a balcony. This is the single most actionable step to prevent
generator-related carbon monoxide poisoning, since a generator running
even a short distance from an opening can still allow exhaust to enter
a shelter.

**Carbon monoxide alarms are a last line of defense, not a
replacement for the rules above.** Install carbon monoxide alarms in a
central location outside each separate sleeping area, and on every
level of the shelter. An alarm can catch a problem that the placement
rules above failed to prevent, but should never be treated as
permission to place a generator or other fuel-burning device somewhere
those rules already prohibit.

**A vented fireplace or wood stove is a legitimate heating option, but
only with several precautions followed together.** If using one: have
flues and chimneys inspected before each heating season for leakage or
blockage, open the damper before lighting a fire and keep it open
until the ashes are fully cool, never close the damper or leave the
space with warm ashes still present, never burn charcoal in a
fireplace (a real carbon monoxide risk many people don't expect from
what looks like a controlled, ventilated fire), and burn only dry,
seasoned wood. These five precautions work together - skipping one
while following the rest still leaves a real gap.

**A battery or solar-powered alternative avoids this entire category
of risk.** Because they don't burn fuel, battery or solar-powered
generators produce no toxic fumes and can be used more safely indoors
or near windows than a fuel-burning generator - a real, useful
alternative wherever it's available, rather than a full substitute for
following the fuel-generator placement rules above when a fuel
generator is the only option on hand.

**Space heaters need their own clearance and supervision rules,
separate from the fire-fuel-and-CO concerns above.** Keep anything
that can burn at least 3 feet away from any heat source - space
heaters, fireplaces, wood stoves, radiators, or candles alike - and
turn a space heater off whenever leaving the room or going to sleep.
Never use an oven to try to heat a shelter.

**Planning ahead reduces how much heating is needed in the first
place.** In cold climates, a stove with roughly 5 to 7 kilowatts of
heating capacity is a commonly used planning figure for heating a
floor area of roughly 40 to 70 square meters - a useful planning
number for matching a stove to a shelter's size, not a guarantee for
any specific stove or shelter. Reducing how much space needs heating,
and how leaky a shelter is, reduces the fuel and heating capacity
needed overall.

**Fire risk isn't only about individual devices - it's also about how
shelters are arranged.** At settlement scale, the spacing and firebreak
figures already covered in "Shelter Site Selection and System
Planning" exist specifically so that a fire in one shelter - however
it starts - has a much harder time reaching the next one.

None of this replaces the basic open-fire safety practices already
covered in "Emergency Fire Safety" (clearing a burn area, keeping
water or dirt nearby, never leaving a fire unattended, and fully
extinguishing it) - those apply just as much to any open fire used
near or around a shelter. This article covers the additional,
distinct risks that come specifically from heating an enclosed
occupied space. If someone in a shelter shows signs of dangerously low
body temperature despite these precautions, see "Hypothermia and
Heatstroke Field Response" for recognizing and responding to it - that
is a medical response question, separate from the prevention steps
covered here.

## Benefits

- Distinguishes several genuinely different equipment categories -
  fuel devices, generators, fireplaces, space heaters - rather than
  collapsing them into one vague warning
- Names carbon monoxide risk specifically, including the
  less-obvious charcoal-in-a-fireplace case many people don't expect
- Gives a real, safer alternative (battery/solar power) alongside the
  rules for when only fuel-burning options are available
- Connects individual-shelter thermal safety to settlement-scale fire
  spacing, so the two reinforce rather than duplicate each other
