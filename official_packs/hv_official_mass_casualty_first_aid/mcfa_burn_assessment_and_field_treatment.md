# Burn Assessment and Field Treatment

## Summary

Cooling a burn promptly with clean, cool running water - and avoiding several common but harmful home remedies - are widely recognized first steps for a thermal burn. This article covers general burn first aid; electrical and chemical burns need different handling, and any burn beyond a small, minor one needs professional medical care.

## Main Content

For an ordinary thermal burn (from heat, not electricity or chemicals), first remove the person from whatever caused the burn and remove any clothing or jewelry near the burned area that isn't stuck to the skin. Cool the burned area with clean, running water. Avoid using ice directly on a burn, since ice can damage already-injured tissue further, and avoid cooling for so long that the person becomes uncomfortably cold - the goal is to cool the burn, not to cause a new problem from over-cooling. If in doubt about how long to cool a burn, err toward the shorter, gentler end and seek guidance from a medical provider or the official source listed below.

Several traditional home remedies should not be used on a burn: do not apply oils, butter, toothpaste, or similar substances, and do not apply raw materials like flour or turmeric. These do not help and can introduce infection or trap heat against the skin. Cover a cooled burn loosely with a clean, non-stick cloth rather than leaving it exposed or wrapping it tightly.

Do not deliberately break or drain a blister. An intact blister helps protect the healing skin underneath; if it breaks on its own, keep the area as clean as possible and watch for signs of infection.

Electrical burns and chemical burns are different from ordinary thermal burns and need different first steps. For an electrical burn, first make sure the power source is actually off before touching the person - a live electrical source can injure a rescuer too - and be aware that an electrical injury can affect the heart even when the visible burn looks minor, so professional evaluation is important even for a small-looking electrical burn. For a chemical burn, the general approach is to remove contaminated clothing and flush the area with large amounts of clean water, rather than simply cooling it the way a thermal burn would be cooled - but exact guidance can vary by the specific chemical involved, so this article does not attempt to cover chemical-specific handling.

Any burn that is large, deep, on the face, hands, or genitals, caused by electricity or chemicals, or that shows signs of infection needs professional medical care - field first aid is meant to limit damage while that care is arranged, not to replace it.

## Benefits

- Cooling promptly can reduce how deep and severe a burn becomes
- Avoiding harmful home remedies prevents making the injury worse
- Recognizing when electrical or chemical burns need different handling can prevent a rescuer from being injured too
