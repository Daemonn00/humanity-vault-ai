# Fracture and Sprain Stabilization

## Summary

It is often impossible to tell a fracture, sprain, or dislocation apart just by looking, so the safest approach is to treat any suspected bone or joint injury the same way: keep it still, avoid unnecessary movement, and seek professional care. This article covers general recognition and stabilization principles; it is not a substitute for first aid training.

## Main Content

A fracture (a broken bone), a sprain (a stretched or torn ligament), and a dislocation (a joint forced out of its normal position) can look and feel similar in the field - swelling, pain, and an inability to use the injured area normally can all occur with any of the three. Rather than trying to diagnose exactly which one has happened, the safer approach is to treat the injury as if it could be a fracture, and let a trained provider make the actual diagnosis later.

Do not move a person with a suspected spine injury. Do not move a person with an injured hip, pelvis, or upper leg unless it is absolutely necessary - if you must move them, pull them to safety by their clothes (the shoulders of a shirt, a belt, or pant legs) rather than lifting or carrying them. If there is no safety reason to move the person, keep them still, keep them calm, and get professional medical help as soon as possible - call for emergency help any time a broken bone in the head, neck, back, hip, pelvis, or upper leg is suspected.

For a suspected fracture, sprain, or dislocation in a limb, the general goal of stabilization is to keep the injured area from moving. Find something rigid to use as a splint - sticks, boards, or even rolled-up newspaper; if nothing rigid is available, a rolled blanket or clothing can work, or the injured part can be taped to an uninjured part next to it (for example, taping an injured finger to the one beside it). The splint should extend beyond the injured area on both sides so it can't move. Check the area often for swelling, paleness, or numbness, and seek medical help right away if there is loss of pulse or warmth, or if fingers or toes turn blue and lose feeling.

None of this is a substitute for professional evaluation. The goal of field stabilization is to avoid making an injury worse while arranging for real medical care, not to treat the injury completely.

## Benefits

- Reduces the risk of making an injury worse through unnecessary movement
- Can be done with improvised materials when no medical supplies are available
- Buys time and reduces pain and further damage until professional care is reached
