# Hypothermia and Heatstroke Field Response

## Summary

Hypothermia (dangerously low body temperature) and heatstroke (dangerously high body temperature) are both medical emergencies, and both have a common mistake to avoid: rewarming a hypothermic person too quickly, or giving fluids to someone with heatstroke who is confused or losing consciousness, can make things worse rather than better.

## Main Content

Hypothermia develops when the body loses heat faster than it can produce it, most often from cold, wet, or windy conditions, especially over a long exposure. Early signs include shivering and feeling cold; as it worsens, a person may become confused, clumsy, or unusually slow to respond, and in severe cases can lose consciousness. Because hypothermia affects thinking, the person may not realize how serious it has become, so others around them need to watch for it too.

If you suspect hypothermia, move the person out of the cold and away from wind as soon as possible. Gently remove any wet clothing and replace it with dry insulation - blankets, dry coats, anything available. Focus on warming the body's core (torso) rather than just the arms and legs. If the person is alert enough to swallow safely, warm (not hot) sweet, non-alcoholic drinks can help. Avoid rapid rewarming methods - such as a hot bath or direct high heat - since warming a cold body too quickly is a recognized risk, not a shortcut to recovery. If the person shows no signs of life, begin CPR if you are trained to do so, and get emergency help as soon as possible either way.

Heatstroke is the most serious heat-related illness. It happens when the body's cooling system (sweating) fails and body temperature rises to a dangerous level, sometimes very quickly. Warning signs include a high body temperature, hot and possibly dry skin (though heavy sweating can also occur), confusion or unusual behavior, slurred speech, a fast pulse, and nausea. Confusion or loss of consciousness is a sign of a genuine emergency, not just discomfort from the heat.

If you suspect heatstroke, treat it as a medical emergency and get professional help immediately. Move the person to a cooler place if possible, and begin cooling them - wet cloths, fanning, or immersing them in cool water if that's safely possible are all reasonable approaches while waiting for help. Do not give fluids to someone who is confused or losing consciousness - they may not be able to swallow safely, and fluid going into the airway instead of the stomach is a real danger. If the person is fully alert and able to swallow normally, that's a different, less severe situation, but any suspected heatstroke still needs professional evaluation.

Both conditions share a common thread: the instinct to "fix it fast" (a hot bath for someone freezing, a large drink for someone overheated and confused) can be the wrong move. Gentle, gradual care while getting professional help is safer than an aggressive fix in either direction.

## Benefits

- Recognizing early signs of both conditions can prompt faster, safer action
- Knowing what not to do (rapid rewarming, giving fluids to a confused person) avoids two commonly-made mistakes
