# Shock Recognition and Management

## Summary

Shock is a life-threatening condition where the body can't deliver enough blood and oxygen to its organs, and it can develop after any serious injury or illness, not just heavy bleeding. Recognizing the signs early and getting professional help quickly matters more than any specific field treatment.

## Main Content

Shock can follow severe bleeding, a serious burn, a severe allergic reaction, a bad infection, or a number of other serious injuries or illnesses - it is not limited to situations with visible blood loss. Because it can develop gradually and worsen quickly, watching for it in anyone who has a serious injury is important even if they seem stable at first.

Signs that may indicate shock include: skin that is unusually cool, clammy, or pale; a fast, weak pulse; fast, shallow breathing; nausea; unusual thirst; confusion, anxiety, or restlessness; and, as it worsens, dizziness or fainting. Someone showing several of these signs after an injury needs urgent medical attention.

While waiting for professional help, keep the person as calm and reassured as possible - fear and pain can make shock worse. Keep them from getting too cold or too hot. Do not give them food or water, even if they ask for it, since shock can affect digestion and safe swallowing, and they may need medical procedures soon that are safer on an empty stomach.

Positioning a person in shock is a case where the details matter and can depend on what else is wrong with them - for example, a suspected spinal injury changes what's safe to do. This article does not give a single specific positioning instruction to follow in every case; if you have had first aid training, follow it, and otherwise keep the person still, comfortable, and as calm as possible while you get professional help, rather than moving them into a specific position you're unsure about.

Shock is always a medical emergency. Field care can help avoid making it worse while professional help is on the way, but it cannot treat the underlying cause.

## Benefits

- Recognizing shock early, even before someone looks seriously unwell, can prompt faster action
- Simple comfort and reassurance measures can help without any equipment or training
- Understanding that shock isn't limited to bleeding injuries helps you watch for it after burns, severe illness, or other trauma too
