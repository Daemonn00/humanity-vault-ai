# Shock Recognition and Management

## Summary

Shock is a life-threatening condition where the body can't deliver enough blood and oxygen to its organs, and it can develop after any serious injury or illness, not just heavy bleeding. Recognizing the signs early and getting professional help quickly matters more than any specific field treatment.

## Main Content

Shock can follow heart problems, low blood volume (from heavy bleeding or dehydration), severe allergic reactions, infection, or a number of other serious conditions - it is not limited to situations with visible blood loss. Because it can develop gradually and worsen quickly, watching for it in anyone who has a serious injury or illness is important even if they seem stable at first.

Signs that may indicate shock include: skin that is unusually cool, clammy, or pale; a fast, weak pulse; fast, shallow breathing; nausea; unusual thirst; confusion, anxiety, or restlessness; and, as it worsens, dizziness or fainting. Someone showing several of these signs after an injury needs urgent medical attention.

While waiting for professional help, keep the person as calm and reassured as possible - fear and pain can make shock worse. Loosen any tight clothing and keep them warm and comfortable. Do not give the person anything by mouth, including anything to eat or drink.

If the person is conscious and does not have an injury to the head, leg, neck, or spine, lay them on their back and elevate their legs about 12 inches (30 centimeters) - do not elevate their head. If a spine injury is suspected and the person vomits, keep the head, neck, and back aligned and roll them onto their side as one unit rather than turning just the head. If you do not suspect a spine injury and the person is not fully alert, turning their head to one side can help prevent choking.

Shock is always a medical emergency. Field care can help avoid making it worse while professional help is on the way, but it cannot treat the underlying cause.

## Benefits

- Recognizing shock early, even before someone looks seriously unwell, can prompt faster action
- Simple comfort and reassurance measures can help without any equipment or training
- Understanding that shock isn't limited to bleeding injuries helps you watch for it after severe illness or other serious conditions too
