# Triage Basics for Multiple Casualties

## Summary

Triage is the practice of sorting injured people by how urgently they need help when there are more people hurt than can be treated one at a time. This article explains why triage exists and the general principle behind it; it does not provide a specific step-by-step assessment procedure, which should come from proper first aid training or the official guidance referenced in Sources.

## Main Content

When an incident injures more people than there are hands or supplies to treat them immediately, treating whoever is closest or loudest first can mean someone with a more urgent, more treatable injury doesn't get help in time. Triage exists to prevent this: it is a structured way of quickly sorting injured people into priority groups, so that care goes first to those who need it most urgently and are most likely to benefit from it.

Formal triage systems exist and are taught to emergency responders precisely because getting this sorting right, quickly, under pressure, is difficult to do well by instinct alone. These systems typically group people into a small number of categories - for example, those needing immediate life-saving care, those who can safely wait a short time, and those with minor injuries who can wait longer or care for themselves - based on a quick check of things like whether someone can walk, whether they are breathing, and whether they respond to simple instructions.

This article deliberately does not attempt to teach a specific triage checklist or category system in detail. Multiple named systems exist, each with their own precise steps and terminology, and using the wrong sequence or getting a threshold wrong could lead to a mis-sorted decision. Anyone who may need to perform triage in a real emergency should seek proper first aid or emergency response training beforehand, and should consult official guidance - such as the source listed below - directly rather than relying on a summary.

Triage is a tool for genuinely overwhelming situations with more injured people than can be helped at once. It is not a substitute for calling emergency services as soon as possible, and it does not apply in an ordinary situation where you are caring for one injured person at a time.

## Benefits

- Helps the most people possible when resources or hands are limited
- Reduces the chance that a less obvious but more urgent injury is missed
- Gives everyone present a shared, structured way to make hard decisions under pressure, rather than reacting only to who is closest or loudest
