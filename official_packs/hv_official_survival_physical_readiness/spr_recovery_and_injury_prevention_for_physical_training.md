# Recovery and Injury Prevention for Physical Training

## Summary

Recovery is a required part of building physical capability, not time off from it - and knowing the difference between ordinary muscle soreness and a warning sign of real injury helps you train safely instead of guessing. This article covers both, and points to existing Humanity Vault content for what to do if an actual medical emergency occurs.

## Main Content

Adaptation - the process behind every capability covered in this pack - depends on balancing physical demand with enough recovery afterward. Training that combines excessive demand with inadequate recovery works against progress rather than for it. A moderate, temporary increase in fatigue that improves performance once you've recovered from it is a normal and expected part of building capability. Insufficient recovery relative to the demand placed on the body is a real, recognized risk, with effects - persistent fatigue, decline in performance, and mood changes - that are distinct from the ordinary tiredness of a single hard day. If you notice this pattern building over time rather than settling with rest, that's a signal to reduce demand and prioritize recovery, not to push through it.

It's normal to feel some muscle discomfort after unfamiliar or more intense physical activity than you're used to. This kind of general, whole-muscle soreness is a normal part of training and typically improves with rest, gentle movement, and time. It is different from a warning sign of an actual injury, and knowing the difference matters. Stop the activity and contact a healthcare provider if muscle pain lasts more than about three days, is severe or unexplained, or comes with signs of infection such as swelling or redness. Some signs call for calling for emergency help right away rather than just arranging a routine evaluation: muscle weakness or an inability to move part of your body normally, sudden unexplained swelling or fluid retention, a marked drop in how much you're urinating, or difficulty breathing or swallowing. If you're ever genuinely unsure which category applies, the safer choice is to treat it as a warning sign and seek help, not to assume it's ordinary soreness and continue.

This article covers preventing training injury, not treating one that has already happened, and it does not repeat guidance already covered elsewhere in Humanity Vault. If you suspect an actual fracture or sprain, see the Mass Casualty & Trauma First Aid pack's fracture and sprain content. If heat or cold exposure during physical work leads to symptoms of heat illness or hypothermia, see that same pack's hypothermia and heatstroke content. For dehydration during physical activity, see the core Vault's dehydration article. This pack's role is helping you avoid needing that care in the first place, through gradual progression and paying attention to your body's warning signs - not replacing it once something has actually gone wrong.
