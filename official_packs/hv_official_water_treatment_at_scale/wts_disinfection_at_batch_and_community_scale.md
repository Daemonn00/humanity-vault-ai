# Disinfection at Batch and Community Scale

## Summary

Disinfecting water for a group is not a single number to memorize - it
is a short connected procedure: the right chlorine dose depends on
the water itself, that dose needs enough contact time to work, and the
result has to be measured afterward to confirm it actually worked.
Skipping any one of these steps can leave water either under-treated
or, in the other direction, dosed with more chlorine than intended.

## Main Content

Chlorine disinfection at batch or community scale follows one
connected sequence, not a set of separate numbers to look up
independently: the water's own condition determines the dose, the
dose needs a minimum contact time to work (adjusted for water
temperature and acidity), and the result must be measured afterward as
free residual chlorine before the water is considered ready to
distribute or drink.

**Determining the dose.** The correct chlorine dose depends on how
turbid (cloudy) the water is and on its "chlorine demand" - the amount
of chlorine that gets used up reacting with organic matter and other
substances in the water before any is left over to actually disinfect
it. Because this varies by source, the dose is normally determined
experimentally with a side-by-side dosing test on samples of the
actual water, rather than read off a fixed table. Two technical
references report different example starting figures for this test,
reflecting different organizations' typical guidance rather than one
single correct number: the Global WASH Cluster eCompendium reports
roughly 0.5 to 2 milligrams per liter for non-turbid water and up to
about 6 milligrams per liter for turbid water; Oxfam WASH reports
roughly 2.0 milligrams per liter for water below about 10 NTU
turbidity and roughly 4.0 to 5.0 milligrams per liter for water in the
10-100 NTU range. These figures are useful starting points for a
dosing test, not a number to apply directly without testing the actual
water.

**Contact time.** Once dosed, chlorine needs time to work. A commonly
cited minimum contact time is 30 minutes. Oxfam WASH reports that this
standard applies at around 25°C water temperature - in colder water,
more time is needed, roughly doubling for every 10°C drop in
temperature - and that the water's acidity also matters: chlorine
disinfects most effectively below a pH of about 8; above that, either
a higher chlorine concentration or a longer contact time is needed to
compensate.

**Measuring the result.** After the contact time has passed, the
amount of chlorine still present - the free residual chlorine, or
FRC - is what confirms whether disinfection actually worked, and this
target is not one single number everywhere in the system. Oxfam WASH
distinguishes three separate points: at a distribution point itself,
an operational target in the rough range of 0.5 to 1.2 milligrams per
liter (higher when conditions are poor or temperatures are high) helps
ensure enough chlorine survives the journey to reach households; at
the point of delivery, a minimum of 0.2 milligrams per liter free
residual chlorine is reported; and that same 0.2 milligrams per liter
minimum is also reported for what should still be present in the
water at the actual point of consumption - the last cup poured at the
household. If a reading comes back low at any of these points, that
signals more chlorine or more contact time is needed - the sequence
loops back rather than ending.

There is also an upper limit: Oxfam WASH reports that free residual
chlorine should never exceed roughly 4.0 to 5.0 milligrams per liter.
The Global WASH Cluster eCompendium does not itself state a specific
upper number, only that chlorine taste can cause people to reject the
water - a real concern well below this ceiling, since many people find
chlorine's taste and smell noticeable at levels much lower than the
upper limit.

**Point-of-supply hardware.** Where chlorine is dosed automatically at
a community water point rather than by hand, tablet-based or liquid
dosing devices are commonly used. These release chlorine at a
concentration of roughly 2 milligrams per liter, adjustable to the
water's actual chlorine demand, with typical device flow rates around
10 to 30 liters per minute. A single chlorine tablet can commonly treat
somewhere in the range of 1,500 to 2,500 liters of water, though this
varies by manufacturer, device, and tablet strength.

**Water quality affects the whole sequence.** Water with high organic
content can react with chlorine to form harmful by-products - this is
one more reason pre-treating turbid or organic-rich water (see
"Pre-Treating Water at Community Scale") before chlorinating it
matters, rather than trying to chlorinate around the problem with a
larger dose.

Because dosing, contact time, and residual measurement are one
connected process rather than independent facts, a group setting up
community-scale disinfection should treat this as a procedure to
follow and monitor over time, not a single number to apply once.

## Benefits

- Disinfection is the step that most directly protects against
  disease-causing organisms that filtration alone may not fully remove
- Measuring free residual chlorine gives a direct way to confirm
  treatment actually worked, rather than assuming it did
- The dosing-test approach adapts to the actual water being treated,
  rather than relying on a number that may not fit local conditions
- Point-of-supply hardware can maintain consistent dosing at a shared
  water point without requiring manual dosing for every batch
