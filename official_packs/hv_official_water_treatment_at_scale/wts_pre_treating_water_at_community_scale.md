# Pre-Treating Water at Community Scale

## Summary

Cloudy (turbid) water needs to be cleared before it can be filtered or
disinfected effectively. Pre-treatment - letting particles settle,
sometimes with the help of a coagulant chemical, or passing very
turbid water through a coarse gravel filter first - reduces cloudiness
to a level the next treatment stage can actually handle.

## Main Content

Sedimentation is the simplest pre-treatment step: suspended particles
in water settle out over time under gravity alone. A practical field
test is to let a sample settle for about an hour (or however long the
planned settling time will be) and check whether it has cleared
enough - if the top portion of the container is clear enough to move
on to the next treatment step, plain settling is doing the job.

For more turbid water, a coagulant chemical can speed this process up
by helping fine particles clump together and settle faster. Aluminum
sulfate is a commonly used coagulant, and it works best in water with
a pH of roughly 6 to 8. It also has a health-based limit: the aluminum
remaining in the clarified water should not exceed 0.2 milligrams per
liter. If this limit is a concern, it can be addressed by adjusting
the raw water's pH or by passing the water through a rapid sand filter
afterward. The correct coagulant dose for a specific batch of water
cannot be worked out from a table - it is determined experimentally,
with a side-by-side test using a series of samples dosed at
increasing coagulant levels to find the dose that actually clarifies
that water.

For water that is very turbid - well beyond what plain sedimentation
can handle - roughing filtration is another pre-treatment option: raw
water passes through a bed of graded gravel (coarser stones near the
inlet, finer near the outlet), physically straining out much of the
suspended material. This method can bring quite muddy water down to a
level suitable for a slower, finer filtration step afterward. Roughing
filtration is a pre-treatment step only, not a substitute for
disinfection - it reduces particles and some of the bacteria carried
with them, but it does not reliably make water safe to drink on its
own.

The practical goal of pre-treatment, whichever method is used, is to
reduce turbidity to a level the next step in the treatment chain can
handle - filtration and disinfection both work better, and more
predictably, on clearer water. Exactly how clear "clear enough" needs
to be depends on which treatment comes next.

## Benefits

- Makes filtration and disinfection more effective and more
  predictable by removing the particles that interfere with both
- Can be done with minimal equipment - a settling container, and
  optionally a coagulant and a simple gravel filter
- Provides a way to handle water too turbid for filtration or
  disinfection to treat safely on their own
- The settling/jar-test approach lets a group calibrate treatment to
  their actual water, rather than guessing
