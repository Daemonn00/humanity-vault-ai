# Safe Storage, Distribution, and Preventing Recontamination

## Summary

Treating water correctly does not help if it becomes contaminated
again before it reaches someone's cup. Community-scale storage tanks
and distribution networks need their own design and maintenance
attention to prevent that from happening. This article covers shared
tanks and pipe networks serving multiple households - for a single
household's own storage containers, see "Water Storage and Long-Term
Water Safety."

## Main Content

Community storage tanks range widely in size - from roughly 10 liters
up to 1,000 liters or more - and are built from a range of materials
depending on what's available and affordable, including plastic,
brick or concrete, ferrocement, or metal with a protective lining. A
tank should be sized to at least meet the group's daily demand plus a
contingency margin - commonly at least one day's worth of extra
capacity, and up to three days where the water supply itself is less
predictable, such as with solar- or wind-powered pumping.

Preventing contamination inside the tank matters as much as the
tank's size. Tanks need a screened ventilation opening to prevent
pressure or vacuum from building up as water levels change, and
screens on the inlet, outlet, and overflow pipes to keep insects out
and prevent them breeding inside. Periodic cleaning is also part of
normal operation, with frequency depending on the water's quality
going in - cleaner source water needs less frequent cleaning than
turbid or poorly-treated water. As part of routine maintenance, a much
higher chlorine concentration than the routine drinking-water doses
used for actual disinfection (see "Disinfection at Batch and Community
Scale") - reported at around 50 milligrams per liter - can be used
specifically to clean and disinfect a tank's interior. Because this
concentration is far higher than water intended for drinking (tens of
times higher than the doses used for routine disinfection), a tank
treated this way should not be returned to normal use until it has
been thoroughly flushed and is confirmed to be back within normal
drinking-water disinfection levels - this is a common-sense precaution
given how much higher the cleaning concentration is, not a specific
flushing procedure recommended by name in this article's sources.

Distribution networks - the pipes and tap stands that carry water from
storage to households - have their own design considerations. A
network is generally planned around a minimum residual pressure of
about 5 meters at the highest or farthest tap, with a target minimum
flow of around 0.125 liters per second at each tap, serving no more
than around 250 people per tap (the same planning figures introduced
in "Assessing Community Water Needs and Protecting the Source," here
applied specifically to the pipe network itself). Cross-contamination
is a real risk in these systems: when a network operates
intermittently rather than continuously, pressure can drop or briefly
reverse at leaky joints, drawing contaminated water in from outside
the pipe rather than pushing clean water out. Careful installation -
correct trenching, bedding, and backfilling - reduces leaks in the
first place, and water pipes should always be laid above any
wastewater pipes in the same area to reduce the risk of the two
crossing paths if either is damaged.

## Benefits

- Protects the investment already made in treating the water, by
  preventing it from being recontaminated afterward
- Gives concrete design targets (tank sizing, pressure, screening) a
  community can plan and build toward
- Names specific, preventable failure points (intermittent operation,
  pipe crossing, insect entry) rather than leaving contamination risk
  vague
- Complements household-level storage guidance rather than duplicating
  it, for systems serving more than one household
