# Human Verification Workbook (Pre-Drafting) — Mass Casualty & Trauma First Aid

**This workbook was produced before any article was written**, based
on the planned claims in `mass_casualty_first_aid_RESEARCH_PLAN.md`
and the confirmed sources in `mass_casualty_first_aid_SOURCE_
COLLECTION.md` - not on drafted article prose, since none exists yet.
This is a deliberate departure from how the first workbook in this
project (`field_communication_wayfinding_HUMAN_VERIFICATION_
WORKBOOK.md`) was built, which worked backward from already-written
articles.

**Consequence of this ordering, stated plainly:** once Stage 4
(Writing) happens, the actual article sentences may phrase things
differently than the "planned claim" wording below. **This workbook
must be re-checked against the real article text once drafted** -
treat the claims below as a verification target for drafting to hit,
not a guarantee that the final article text will match word-for-word.
This is the correct, intended use of doing verification prep before
writing: it tells the drafter exactly what must be directly
supportable before a single sentence is written, rather than
discovering a gap after the fact.

**File placement note:** sibling of `official_packs/`, same safe
pattern as the Field Comm workbook - see that file's own header for
why.

## Article 1: Triage Basics for Multiple Casualties

**Source:** CHEMM Triage Guidelines (chemm.hhs.gov/triage.htm)

| # | Planned claim | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| T1 | A simple triage system exists for situations with more injured people than can be treated one at a time | Important |
| T2 | Triage sorts people into a small number of priority categories based on urgency and likelihood treatment will help | Important |
| T3 | A quick first check (responsiveness/breathing/ability to follow instructions) is enough for initial sorting without formal medical training | **Critical** - this is the article's actual actionable instruction |

**Quotation/confirmation/removability:**
- T1, T2: factual confirmation is sufficient - these are the
  foundational, widely-documented premise of triage as a concept.
- T3: **needs close reading, not just confirmation** - the exact
  check-sequence (what to assess, in what order) must match a real
  named system's actual steps, not a generic paraphrase, since this is
  literally the instruction a reader would follow. If CHEMM's own
  description differs from the version researched, use CHEMM's version
  exactly.
- If T3 can't be confirmed precisely: narrow the article to explaining
  *why* triage sorting exists and how priority categories generally
  work, without giving a specific do-it-yourself assessment sequence -
  safer than publishing an unconfirmed, actionable medical procedure.

**Estimated verification effort:** ~30-45 minutes (one source, but a
technical government resource, and T3 warrants a careful, slow read).

## Article 2: Fracture and Sprain Stabilization

**Sources:** American Red Cross, "Fractures" + "Muscle, Bone and Joint
Injury"

| # | Planned claim | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| F1 | Often can't reliably tell fracture from sprain/dislocation in the field - treat suspected injuries the same way | Important |
| F2 | Suspected head, neck, back, or pelvis injury: do not move the person unless there's a more urgent safety reason | **Critical** - highest-stakes single claim in this pack alongside T3 |
| F3 | Basic splinting: immobilize the joint above and below the injury; improvised materials can substitute for commercial splints | Important |

**Quotation/confirmation/removability:**
- F1: factual confirmation sufficient.
- F2: **needs close, exact reading** - "unless there's a more urgent
  safety reason" is doing a lot of work in this sentence (e.g. fire,
  drowning risk) and must match the Red Cross source's own stated
  exceptions exactly, not be loosened or tightened by paraphrase.
- F3: factual confirmation sufficient for the general principle;
  specific improvised-material examples should be checked against
  what the source actually suggests before being named specifically.
- If F2 can't be confirmed precisely: this claim is **not removable**
  (it's the article's core safety warning) - if the exact exception
  wording can't be confirmed, state the "do not move" instruction
  without any exception clause at all, which is more conservative and
  therefore safer to publish unconfirmed than a loosened version.

**Estimated verification effort:** ~30-40 minutes (two related,
consumer-facing Red Cross pages - likely faster reading than Article
1's technical source).

## Article 3: Burn Assessment and Field Treatment

**Source:** WHO, "Burns" fact sheet

| # | Planned claim | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| B1 | Cool a burn with clean running water (not ice) to reduce tissue damage | **Critical** - flagged cooling-duration nuance |
| B2 | Certain home remedies (oils, pastes, ice, butter) should never be applied to a burn | Important |
| B3 | Blisters should not be deliberately opened in the field | Important |
| B4 | Electrical and chemical burns need different handling than thermal burns | **Critical** - electrical burns can have hidden internal effects |

**Quotation/confirmation/removability:**
- B1: **needs close, exact reading of duration/technique wording** -
  Source Collection already flagged that WHO's own fact sheet
  cautions against "prolonged" cooling (hypothermia risk), which is
  more nuanced than a flat "cool for 20 minutes" instruction seen
  elsewhere in initial research. The article must use WHO's own
  framing exactly, not a generic figure from a different source.
- B2, B3: factual confirmation sufficient - these were unambiguous in
  initial research.
- B4: factual confirmation sufficient for the general principle
  (different handling needed); if WHO's fact sheet doesn't cover
  electrical/chemical burns in enough depth, this claim may need a
  second, specifically-electrical/chemical-burn source rather than
  being stretched from a thermal-burn-focused document.
- If B1's exact duration guidance can't be pinned down: use WHO's own
  words directly ("cool with running water, avoid prolonging it")
  rather than inventing a specific minute figure not stated in the
  source.

**Estimated verification effort:** ~25-35 minutes (one source, but B1
requires deliberate, careful reading, not skimming).

## Article 4: Shock Recognition and Management

**Sources:** American Red Cross "Shock" + 2024 AHA/Red Cross Guidelines

| # | Planned claim | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | Shock can develop after any serious injury or illness, not just visible blood loss | Important |
| S2 | Recognizable signs: cool/clammy/pale skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, confusion/anxiety | Important |
| S3 | Field positioning (lying down, legs elevated if no trauma contraindication) can help | **Critical** - positioning guidance noted as possibly conditional/shifting in recent updates |
| S4 | Do not give food or drink to someone showing signs of shock | Important |

**Quotation/confirmation/removability:**
- S1, S2, S4: factual confirmation sufficient.
- S3: **needs close reading of both sources, cross-checked against
  each other** - Source Collection flagged that positioning
  recommendations may be conditional (trauma vs. non-trauma) or have
  shifted in the 2024 clinical update; the article must reflect
  whatever nuance/exception the sources actually state, not a
  simplified flat instruction.
- If S3's positioning guidance is genuinely conditional/complex: state
  the condition explicitly (e.g. "only if there's no sign of injury to
  the legs, pelvis, or spine") rather than omitting the nuance, since
  a wrong flat instruction here is worse than a more careful
  conditional one.

**Estimated verification effort:** ~45-60 minutes (two sources, one of
which - the AHA/Red Cross clinical guideline - is a long document
requiring more careful navigation to the relevant section).

## Article 5: Hypothermia and Heatstroke Field Response

**Sources:** CDC "First Aid for Heat Illness"; CDC/NIOSH "Heat-Related
Illnesses"; CDC/NIOSH "Cold-related Illnesses in Workers"

| # | Planned claim | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| H1 | Hypothermia warning signs progress from shivering to confusion/clumsiness to unconsciousness | Important |
| H2 | Hypothermia field response: move out of cold, insulate, remove wet clothing, gradual center-of-body rewarming, **no rapid rewarming** | **Critical** - counter-intuitive safety claim |
| H3 | Heatstroke warning signs: very high body temperature, confusion/altered mental status, hot skin, rapid pulse | Important |
| H4 | Heatstroke response: move to cooler environment, rapid cooling, **do NOT give fluids** if confused/losing consciousness | **Critical** - counter-intuitive safety claim, aspiration risk if wrong |

**Quotation/confirmation/removability:**
- H1, H3: factual confirmation sufficient.
- H2: **needs close reading** - "no rapid rewarming" and "gradual,
  center-of-body-first" are the article's core safety instruction and
  must match the CDC/NIOSH cold-stress source's actual stated method,
  not a generalized version.
- H4: **needs close reading** - the fluids caveat is conditional on
  mental state ("if confused/losing consciousness"), and this
  condition must be stated precisely, matching the CDC source exactly,
  since a reader giving fluids to an unconscious person is a real
  aspiration risk.
- Neither H2 nor H4 is removable if verification is incomplete - these
  are the two claims where following the *wrong* intuitive instinct
  (rapid rewarming, giving fluids) is actively dangerous. If precise
  confirmation isn't possible, keep the most conservative version of
  the warning (i.e., keep the "do not" instruction even if the exact
  supporting detail is trimmed) rather than removing the caution.

**Estimated verification effort:** ~50-70 minutes (three sources
across two related but distinct topics - budget for both halves
separately, not as one continuous read).

time). This principle should guide every fallback decision during
Stage 4 Writing and Stage 6 Verification alike.
